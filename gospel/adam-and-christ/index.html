The Gospel in Romans · Romans 5:12-6:23

Adam, Christ, and the New Humanity

Paul widens the gospel story from individual guilt to two representative heads. Adam's trespass opens the reign of sin and death; Christ's obedience creates a new humanity whose members share His life.

Romans 5:12-14

Sin and Death Enter Through One Man

12Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned: 14Nevertheless death reigned from Adam to Moses, even over them that had not sinned after the similitude of Adam's transgression, who is the figure of him that was to come.

Two Humanities

01 · Sin and Death Enter Through One Man

Adam's act opens a history in which sin reigns and death reaches all. Human beings confirm that solidarity through sins of their own.

Paul now pivots from the blessings of justification to the vast contrast between Adam and Christ. Sin entered the world through one man, and death followed sin. The sentence begins a comparison that will be carried through the following verses, but before Paul completes it he pauses to explain crucial features of the first side of the comparison. The opening claim is unmistakable: Adam's fall brought sin and death into human history. Paul treats this as a real event tied to a real fall. Paul places strong emphasis on the universality of Adam's impact. One person affected all. Adam's sin opened the door through which sin and death entered the human race. Humanity after Adam does not begin from innocence in the same unfallen condition in which he was created. The race is damaged. Death spreads. The pull toward selfishness becomes universal. Even so, this should not be turned into the idea that individuals are personally guilty for Adam's act apart from their own sin. Paul's own wording here points to personal participation: death passed upon all men, "for that all have sinned." That balance matters. Adam's fall affected everybody; no one stands outside its consequences. Yet God's judgment remains morally just.

Romans 5:15-17

The Gift Exceeds the Trespass

15But not as the offence, so also is the free gift. For if through the offence of one many be dead, much more the grace of God, and the gift by grace, which is by one man, Jesus Christ, hath abounded unto many. 17For if by one man's offence death reigned by one; much more they which receive abundance of grace and of the gift of righteousness shall reign in life by one, Jesus Christ.)

Two Humanities

02 · The Gift Exceeds the Trespass

Paul refuses to place Adam and Christ on equal scales. The trespass devastates, but grace and the gift of righteousness abound more.

Paul begins to unfold the superiority of Christ's gift over Adam's trespass. Adam and Christ are comparable in that each acts representatively, but the results are not equal in power or kind. The free gift is not like the offense. That contrast is one of the keys to the whole paragraph. The grace of God is not merely a balancing force that offsets the damage. It overflows beyond it. "For if through the offence of one many be dead, much more the grace of God, and the gift by grace, which is by one man, Jesus Christ, hath abounded unto many." Paul admits the seriousness of Adam's offense without hesitation. The one trespass brought death to the many. The spread of sin and mortality is real and tragic. But then comes the great reversal: "much more." This point is central: Christ is far more powerful to save than Adam was to destroy. The language of gift and grace must not be overlooked. What comes through Christ is not wages, not inheritance by natural descent, and not reward for moral effort. It is gift. And it is gift "by grace." That means its source is wholly in God's favor, not in human deserving.

Romans 5:18-19

Condemnation and Justification of Life

18Therefore as by the offence of one judgment came upon all men to condemnation; even so by the righteousness of one the free gift came upon all men unto justification of life. 19For as by one man's disobedience many were made sinners, so by the obedience of one shall many be made righteous.

Two Humanities

03 · Condemnation and Justification of Life

The disobedience of one and the obedience of One establish opposing realms. Christ answers Adam at the representative root of humanity's ruin.

Paul restates and completes the comparison that began in verse 12. One trespass brought condemnation; one righteous act brings the free gift unto justification of life. The symmetry is intentional, but it must be read in the light of the chapter as a whole. The phrase "all men" appears on both sides of the comparison. This must be handled with care. The fall in Adam reaches the whole race without exception. On the Christ side, the saving provision is likewise universal in scope. Christ's righteous act is not for a narrow class. He is the Savior of the world in provision. Yet the argument must not be read as universalism. The surrounding verses make clear that the gift must be received, and the whole argument of Romans 3 - 5 places faith at the point where justification becomes effective personally. That reading preserves both the largeness of grace and the necessity of faith. Paul is not narrowing the gospel here. He is enlarging it. No one is excluded from the scope of Christ's provision because of race, history, or prior ruin. If Adam's offense reached all, Christ's righteous act is sufficient for all. But it is enjoyed by those who believe. The expression "the righteousness of one" points to Christ's one decisive saving act viewed in connection with His obedient life and sacrificial death.

Romans 5:20-21

Where Sin Abounded, Grace Superabounded

20Moreover the law entered, that the offence might abound. But where sin abounded, grace did much more abound: 21That as sin hath reigned unto death, even so might grace reign through righteousness unto eternal life by Jesus Christ our Lord.

The Reign of Grace

04 · Where Sin Abounded, Grace Superabounded

The law makes trespass visible, but it does not have the final word. Grace reigns through righteousness unto eternal life in Jesus Christ.

Paul brings the law into the argument without making it the problem or treating grace as permission. The law entered not to create sin out of nothing, but to expose sin more fully, to make it stand out in its true ugliness, and to awaken sinners to their need of grace. When Paul says the offense might abound, he is not praising sin or suggesting that God delights in multiplying evil. He means that the presence of law makes transgression clearer and more conscious. What was already wrong is shown to be unmistakably wrong. The point is that when people come face to face with the Ten Commandments, their lawlessness is uncovered and their need of a Savior becomes plain. That is fitting to Paul's argument. Law does not redeem, but it does reveal. This is why law and grace cannot be enemies. Without law, grace becomes vague and sentimental because we lose sight of what needs forgiveness. Without grace, law condemns but cannot save. Paul joins them in proper order: law exposes the offense; grace rises beyond it. The law is holy light; the sinner resists the light. Grace, however, meets the sinner precisely there. Then comes one of the great "much more" sentences of the chapter. Where sin abounded, grace abounded much more.

Romans 6:1-4

Buried and Raised With Christ

3Know ye not, that so many of us as were baptized into Jesus Christ were baptized into his death? 4Therefore we are buried with him by baptism into death: that like as Christ was raised up from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life.

United With Christ

05 · Buried and Raised With Christ

Baptism declares participation in Christ's death and resurrection. Grace therefore introduces a new walk rather than permission to remain unchanged.

Paul moves from death to burial because burial confirms the reality of death. In baptism, the old order is not merely improved; it is marked as buried with Christ. What is buried is not the personality as such, nor the body as though creation were bad, but the old life dominated by sin. The gospel does not rehabilitate the sovereignty of self. It consigns that old regime to the grave with Christ. Yet Paul's sentence is not satisfied with burial. It presses on to resurrection: "that just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life." The "glory of the Father" points to the powerful majesty and saving action of God. The resurrection of Jesus was not a private reversal of tragedy. It was the Father's mighty vindication of the Son and the beginning of a new creation reality. Those united with Christ are brought into that same resurrection sphere. "Newness of life" must not be reduced to moral tidying or external improvement. Paul is speaking of a grace-created life under a new lordship. The believer rises from the burial of the old existence not to resume business as usual, but to walk differently because he now belongs to the risen Christ. The word "walk" is important.

Romans 6:5-11

The Old Humanity Crucified

5For if we have been planted together in the likeness of his death, we shall be also in the likeness of his resurrection: 6Knowing this, that our old man is crucified with him, that the body of sin might be destroyed, that henceforth we should not serve sin.

United With Christ

06 · The Old Humanity Crucified

Union with Christ breaks sin's claim to mastery. Believers reckon themselves dead to sin and alive to God because Christ now lives beyond death.

Paul explains what happened to the old life. The "old man" is crucified with Christ - the former self under sin's rule, the whole person as shaped by rebellion, self-centeredness, and alienation from God. Paul refuses to treat sin as a superficial defect that can be managed. He speaks in crucifixion language. The old life had to be nailed to the cross with Christ because sin's dominion is deadly, not decorative. He continues: "that the body of sin might be done away with, that we should no longer be slaves of sin." The "body of sin" should not be understood as though the physical body were evil in itself. Paul will soon speak of the members of the body as instruments of righteousness offered to God. The problem is not embodiment but the whole person as commandeered by sin. Under the old order, the body became the arena in which sin expressed its mastery. In Christ, that mastery is broken. The end in view is clear: no longer serving sin. This line carefully joins the objective work of Christ and its practical consequence. The old man "was crucified." That is God's action in union with Christ. But the purpose is ethical and experiential: that slavery to sin might cease.

Romans 6:12-14

Present Yourselves to God

12Let not sin therefore reign in your mortal body, that ye should obey it in the lusts thereof. 13Neither yield ye your members as instruments of unrighteousness unto sin: but yield yourselves unto God, as those that are alive from the dead, and your members as instruments of righteousness unto God.

A New Allegiance

07 · Present Yourselves to God

The body becomes an arena of worship. Grace trains believers to refuse sin's rule and offer every faculty as an instrument of righteousness.

Paul continues the same thought in more concrete terms by calling believers to stop presenting their members as instruments of unrighteousness and to present themselves to God. The members are the capacities of the human person - hands, eyes, tongue, imagination, mind, sexuality, strength, time, and energy. Sin needs instruments through which to act. God also claims the human person for His service. The question is not whether these capacities will be used, but to whom they will be yielded. "Present" is a word of availability and surrender. One may hand oneself over to sin, consciously or unconsciously, by permitting the body and mind to become channels of unrighteousness. A person can also hand himself over to God. Paul is not describing a merely inward spirituality detached from daily life. Sanctification reaches the practical use of the whole person. What we say, where we go, what we look at, what we nurture, what we do with our strength - all of it belongs here. The positive side is especially important: present yourselves to God "as being alive from the dead." Christian obedience is resurrection obedience. The believer is not trying to make himself alive by presenting himself to God. He presents himself because grace has already brought him from death to life. That keeps surrender from becoming legalistic striving.

Romans 6:15-20

Two Masters, Two Kinds of Service

16Know ye not, that to whom ye yield yourselves servants to obey, his servants ye are to whom ye obey; whether of sin unto death, or of obedience unto righteousness? 17But God be thanked, that ye were the servants of sin, but ye have obeyed from the heart that form of doctrine which was delivered you.

A New Allegiance

08 · Two Masters, Two Kinds of Service

Freedom is never moral vacancy. The gospel transfers allegiance from sin's slavery to obedience from the heart and service to righteousness.

Paul introduces servant imagery, not to approve human bondage, but to explain the seriousness of spiritual allegiance in terms his readers would immediately understand. No one stands in a neutral moral space. A person becomes the servant of the power to which he yields. Repeated surrender is not accidental; it is allegiance. Paul identifies only two masters: "sin leading to death, or obedience leading to righteousness." The severity of the contrast must not be softened. Sin is not a harmless liberty. It is a lord whose path ends in death. It leads to righteousness because a life surrendered to God begins to take the shape of God's will. This line is especially important because modern people often speak as if freedom means being answerable to no one. Paul would call that an illusion. The human person is always being formed by what he obeys. Desire obeyed becomes habit; habit becomes character; character reveals mastership. Yield yourself to sin, and sin becomes your lord. Yield yourself to God, and obedience begins to show His righteous work in you. Notice that obedience here is not cold externalism. In the flow of the chapter it belongs to grace, union with Christ, and being alive to God. Paul does not set obedience against faith. He treats obedience as the inevitable expression of faith's allegiance.

Romans 6:21-23

The Wages of Sin and the Gift of God

22But now being made free from sin, and become servants to God, ye have your fruit unto holiness, and the end everlasting life. 23For the wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord.

A New Allegiance

09 · The Wages of Sin and the Gift of God

Paul closes the contrast with two outcomes. Sin pays death as wages; God gives eternal life freely in Jesus Christ our Lord.

Paul closes the chapter with one of Scripture's clearest contrasts: sin pays wages, but God gives a gift. The contrast is exact and devastating. Wages are earned. They are what a master owes for service rendered. Sin is such a master. It keeps people at work under its commands, often hiding the true salary until the end. But the payment is not life, pleasure, freedom, or fulfillment. It is death. Sin always pays according to its own nature, and death is what lies within it. That statement should not be heard lightly. Death is not only physical dissolution, though it includes that. It is the whole ruin brought by separation from God. Sin carries death because it tears the creature away from the Creator, and there is no life apart from Him. So Paul is not describing a cruel overreaction on God's part. He is telling the truth about what sin is and what it produces. Sin pays what it has always contained. Over against that stands "the gift of God." Eternal life is not wages. It cannot be earned by moral effort, religious ceremony, spiritual lineage, or even the believer's later obedience. It is gift from beginning to end. Paul could hardly choose a stronger word to defend grace. Where sin deals in earned payment, God deals in undeserved generosity.
