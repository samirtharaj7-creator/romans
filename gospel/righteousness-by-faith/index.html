The Gospel in Romans · Romans 1:16-5:11

Righteousness by Faith

Paul does not present righteousness by faith as a religious shortcut. He first removes every false refuge, then directs sinners to God's gracious action in Christ and the faith that receives His gift without boasting.

Romans 1:16-17

The Gospel Is God's Power

16For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ: for it is the power of God unto salvation to every one that believeth; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek. 17For therein is the righteousness of God revealed from faith to faith: as it is written, The just shall live by faith.

The Thesis

01 · The Gospel Is God's Power

Paul's thesis joins salvation, revelation, righteousness, and faith. The gospel is powerful because God acts through it to save.

"For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ: for it is the power of God unto salvation to every one that believeth; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek." Paul's lack of shame is not personality bravado. It grows out of what the gospel actually is. The world may regard the message of a crucified and risen Savior as weak, foolish, or socially embarrassing, but Paul knows better. He is not ashamed because the gospel is "the power of God unto salvation." It is not religious advice, moral suggestion, or an inspiring symbol. It is God acting through the good news of Christ to save sinners. The word "salvation" here must be allowed its full breadth. Paul is not speaking narrowly of future escape alone. Salvation includes rescue from the guilt of sin, from the power of sin, and ultimately from all the results of sin. The gospel does not merely inform people about possible help; it is God's own saving power at work for those who believe. Faith, therefore, is not human merit that activates a weak message. Faith is the receiving hand by which the sinner lays hold of a divine power already present in the gospel. Paul adds, "to the Jew first, and also to the Greek." This preserves both historical order and universal scope.

Romans 1:18-32

Wrath Against Suppressed Truth

18For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth in unrighteousness; 19Because that which may be known of God is manifest in them; for God hath shewed it unto them.

Need Exposed

02 · Wrath Against Suppressed Truth

Divine wrath answers humanity's refusal of known truth. Idolatry disorders worship, desire, judgment, and the shared life of society.

"For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth in unrighteousness." Paul moves from the revelation of God's righteousness in the gospel to the revelation of God's wrath against sin. The shift is abrupt, but it is not accidental. The gospel can only be understood rightly when the seriousness of sin is felt. If there were no moral ruin, no suppression of truth, no coming judgment, then salvation would be reduced to improvement rather than rescue. The "wrath of God" must be handled carefully. Paul is not describing divine irritation, cruelty, or loss of control. God's wrath is His holy and judicial opposition to evil, and it must not be compared to human passion. In the movement of this chapter it is revealed as God gives persistent rebels over to the path they have chosen, withdrawing the protection and life-giving presence they refuse. Judgment is therefore neither fickle nor arbitrary; it is the solemn consequence of rejecting the Source of life. Evil destroys His creatures, dishonors His glory, corrupts worship, and spreads ruin through His world. For God to be indifferent to sin would not be mercy; it would be moral collapse.

Romans 2:1-29

The Impartial Judgment of God

5But after thy hardness and impenitent heart treasurest up unto thyself wrath against the day of wrath and revelation of the righteous judgment of God; 6Who will render to every man according to his deeds:

Need Exposed

03 · The Impartial Judgment of God

Moral knowledge cannot shelter the person who judges others while resisting truth. God's judgment reaches conduct, motives, and the heart.

Paul states a principle he will not soften: God "will render to every man according to his deeds." That line stands at the center of the chapter's argument about divine impartiality. God does not judge by profession alone, by possession of truth alone, or by outward privilege alone. He judges according to deeds. This does not overturn salvation by grace, but it does expose every attempt to separate grace from transformation. Works are not the ground on which sinners earn acceptance before God. They are the evidence of what a life is serving, loving, and trusting. Paul's concern here is especially directed against religious presumption. If someone imagines that covenant identity, law, or moral knowledge will shield him while his life runs contrary to God, Paul's sentence destroys that illusion. God's judgment reaches the visible pattern of conduct because deeds reveal the true direction of the heart. They show whether a person bows before truth or resists it, whether grace has borne fruit or has been treated as empty language. The chapter itself helps us understand how this fits with the gospel. Paul is not teaching salvation by self-generated merit. Elsewhere in Romans he will insist that righteousness is received by faith. But the faith that receives righteousness is never barren. It does not leave the life untouched.

Romans 3:1-20

Every Mouth Stopped

19Now we know that what things soever the law saith, it saith to them who are under the law: that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God. 20Therefore by the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified in his sight: for by the law is the knowledge of sin.

Need Exposed

04 · Every Mouth Stopped

Scripture gathers the human family under sin so that privilege, law possession, and human works cannot become grounds for boasting before God.

Paul now draws the conclusion of 1:18 - 3:18. "Whatever the law says, it says to them who are under the law: that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God." The point is not that only Jews are guilty because the law spoke first to them. Rather, because the law speaks to those within its sphere, and because its verdict reaches the whole human family, every mouth is silenced. Jews cannot evade its witness; Gentiles are not exempt from its standard. The world stands accountable before God. The image of the stopped mouth is courtroom language. The defendant has nothing left to say. Excuses, comparisons, inherited privilege, moral achievements, and defensive arguments all fail. This is one of the law's holy ministries. It takes away boasting. It forces the sinner out of self-justification. The law does not flatter or negotiate. It speaks with God's own moral authority. Paul's view of the law here is therefore dignified, not dismissive. The problem is not with the law; the problem is with those upon whom it pronounces judgment. The law reveals reality. It does not create guilt arbitrarily; it exposes guilt truly. That is why any gospel that treats the law as an enemy of grace misses Paul's point. Grace needs the law to unmask the sinner's condition.

Romans 3:21-26

God's Righteousness in Christ

23For all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God; 24Being justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus:

Grace Given

05 · God's Righteousness in Christ

Apart from human achievement, God reveals a righteousness witnessed by Scripture and grounded in Christ's redemptive death.

"Being justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus." Here Paul states the heart of the good news. Those who have sinned and fallen short are "justified" - set right with God - not by earned attainments but "freely," as gift. Grace means God acts for sinners without finding in them the merit that would obligate Him. The initiative is His. The ground is not our worthiness but His mercy. Justification here must be heard against the courtroom setting of verses 19 - 20. The guilty are not merely given better advice; they receive a righteous verdict because God has acted in Christ. This verdict is not an empty legal fiction. God does not pretend that sin is harmless, nor does He confuse justification with the whole work of sanctification. He gives a real verdict grounded in Christ's redemption, and the believer enters a new relation in which grace also begins its transforming work. "Redemption" is liberation language. It recalls ransom and release from bondage. Salvation is not a divine gesture floating in abstraction. It is inseparable from the person and saving work of Christ. The statement also guards the church from subtle merit-theology. If justification is free, it cannot be purchased by ceremony, ancestry, effort, zeal, or moral reform.

Romans 3:27-31

Boasting Excluded, the Law Established

27Where is boasting then? It is excluded. By what law? of works? Nay: but by the law of faith. 28Therefore we conclude that a man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law.

Grace Given

06 · Boasting Excluded, the Law Established

Faith leaves no room for self-congratulation. The one God justifies Jews and Gentiles through faith and establishes the law in its proper witness.

"Where is boasting then? It is excluded. By what law? of works? Nay: but by the law of faith." Paul now draws out a first consequence of justification by grace. Boasting is excluded. If righteousness comes through what God has done in Christ and is received by faith, then there is no room left for spiritual pride. The sinner contributes nothing that can become a ground of self-congratulation before God. Paul's language about "law" here likely works in a pointed, rhetorical way. The principle that rules in God's saving act is not a principle of works, where achievement would produce boasting. It is the "law of faith," the order or governing reality in which all rests on God's gracious provision received by trust. Faith does not become another work to brag about. Quite the opposite: faith empties the sinner of boasting because it turns him away from self and toward Christ. This has important implications for both personal and communal life. Religious pride is not a small corruption. It poisons worship, distorts relationships, and divides the people of God. Paul knows the temptations of Jewish boasting, but the principle applies far beyond first-century Judaism. Christians can boast in orthodoxy, morality, discipline, heritage, ministry, sacrifice, knowledge, or distinctive convictions. Romans 3:27 strikes at the root of all such self-exaltation.

Romans 4:1-25

Abraham Believed God

2For if Abraham were justified by works, he hath whereof to glory; but not before God. 3For what saith the scripture? Abraham believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness.

Promise Received

07 · Abraham Believed God

Abraham becomes the father of all who believe because righteousness was counted to him before circumcision and apart from works as merit.

Paul settles the matter by appealing to Scripture: "Abraham believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness." The weight of the wording falls first on Abraham's believing and then on God's counting. Abraham does not create righteousness by believing; he receives righteousness by trusting the One who promises. Faith here is not bare agreement with a religious proposition. Abraham trusted God's word when the promise lay beyond his own power to achieve. He rested the future on God rather than on himself. That is why this text becomes so important for Paul. It shows that the relationship between God and the justified sinner is grounded not in human performance but in personal trust in the divine promise. The statement also shows that righteousness is something reckoned or credited, not manufactured by the sinner from his own moral strength. Paul's concern is not to turn faith into a new work. Faith is not a more acceptable human achievement replacing old achievements. Faith is the empty hand that receives what grace gives. Alongside this, this reckoning is not unreal. The gift is judicial, but it is not imaginary. God does not flatter the sinner. He places the believer in a right standing because He Himself acts in mercy and truth.

Romans 5:1-11

Peace, Hope, and Reconciliation

1Therefore being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ: 2By whom also we have access by faith into this grace wherein we stand, and rejoice in hope of the glory of God.

Promise Received

08 · Peace, Hope, and Reconciliation

The justified now stand in grace. Tribulation cannot cancel hope because God's love has been demonstrated in Christ's death for His enemies.

Paul's opening "therefore" reaches back into the closing words of Romans 4, where Christ was delivered up for our offenses and raised for our justification. Paul is not beginning a new subject detached from what came before. He is drawing out the first great consequences of justification by faith. Because sinners are counted righteous through Christ rather than through their own merit, peace with God is no longer a wish but a reconciled standing. This peace is something deeper than a passing religious feeling. It is not the thrill of an emotional moment, nor the fragile calm that depends on circumstances going well. It is the end of hostility. We were the ones at war with God. We resisted His will, distrusted His character, and lived in conflict with His government. Justification means that the breach has been healed in Christ. That peace is objective before it becomes deeply felt. It stands on the finished work of Jesus - His death and resurrection - not on the changeability of human moods. The point carries weight because many sincere people judge their standing with God by the weather of their emotions. Paul points somewhere firmer. Peace with God rests on Christ's accomplishment and is received by faith. Where faith lays hold of Him, the conscience finds rest because guilt has been dealt with.
