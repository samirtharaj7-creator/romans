The Gospel in Romans · Romans 9-11

Israel and the Nations

Paul addresses Israel with tears rather than abstraction. These chapters defend God's faithfulness, expose every form of presumption, and place Jewish and Gentile hope within the same mercy revealed in Christ.

Romans 9:1-5

Sorrow and Israel's Privileges

1I say the truth in Christ, I lie not, my conscience also bearing me witness in the Holy Ghost, 2That I have great heaviness and continual sorrow in my heart.

The Question of Israel

01 · Sorrow and Israel's Privileges

Paul begins with grief for his own people and honors the gifts entrusted to Israel, culminating in the Messiah according to the flesh.

The truth Paul has just affirmed is now stated plainly: he has "great heaviness and continual sorrow" in his heart. The language is strong and unembarrassed. This is not a passing disappointment or a momentary burden. It is deep, sustained grief. He has just ended Romans 8 with triumphant assurance that nothing can separate believers from the love of God in Christ. Yet that assurance does not make him emotionally insulated. On the contrary, the more clearly he sees the greatness of God's saving grace, the more painfully he feels the tragedy of rejecting it. Strong theology has not cauterized his heart. It has made it tender. There is an important lesson here. Some people think doctrinal seriousness produces emotional coldness. Romans 9 begins by disproving that idea. Paul is as vigorous in mind as anyone in Scripture, yet he is also brokenhearted. He knows what it means for Christ to be the righteousness of God for sinners. He knows what it means to stand justified by grace. He knows, too, the danger of remaining outside that grace while still possessing religious privilege. That is why his sorrow is not theatrical. It flows from reality. He sees the gap between Israel's calling and Israel's present unbelief, and it wounds him. The phrase "in my heart" is also significant.

Romans 9:6-13

The Word of God Has Not Failed

6Not as though the word of God hath taken none effect. For they are not all Israel, which are of Israel: 8That is, They which are the children of the flesh, these are not the children of God: but the children of the promise are counted for the seed.

Promise and Mercy

02 · The Word of God Has Not Failed

God's promise has always advanced by divine calling rather than mere physical descent. Covenant privilege cannot be reduced to ancestry alone.

Having expressed sorrow and honored Israel's privileges, Paul now begins the theological heart of the chapter: "Not as though the word of God hath taken none effect." Israel's unbelief might look like a collapse of God's promise, but Paul refuses that conclusion at once. The failure is not in God's word. The problem is not that God promised and could not fulfill, or that He purposed and was defeated. God's promise has not fallen to the ground. How does Paul defend that? By making a distinction within Israel itself: "For they are not all Israel, which are of Israel." He is not speaking contemptuously of ethnic Israel, nor erasing their covenant role. He is explaining that belonging outwardly to the nation has never, by itself, guaranteed participation in the saving line of promise. The history of Israel has always involved this distinction between the larger physical people and the inner line of promise shaped by God's calling. That is why Israel's widespread unbelief does not prove that God failed. His saving purpose was never secured merely by physical descent. This is a searching word for every generation. People naturally trust in visible belonging - heritage, community, rites, knowledge, and spiritual culture. Paul says that none of these, by themselves, settle the matter.

Romans 9:14-29

Mercy Belongs to God

15For he saith to Moses, I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I will have compassion. 16So then it is not of him that willeth, nor of him that runneth, but of God that sheweth mercy.

Promise and Mercy

03 · Mercy Belongs to God

God is not indebted to human willing or status. His freedom is the freedom to show mercy and preserve a people who could not save themselves.

Paul draws the conclusion: "So then it is not of him that willeth, nor of him that runneth, but of God that sheweth mercy." Human willing and running stand for desire and effort, intention and exertion. Paul is not saying that human response is irrelevant in every sense, nor denying that faith, obedience, and persevering prayer matter. He is saying that none of these constitute the source or basis of salvation. The decisive cause is not human initiative but divine mercy. That is consistent with the whole movement of Romans. Sinners do respond, believe, repent, confess, and walk in newness of life. But all of that is fruit of grace, not the foundation beneath it. The human will cannot produce saving righteousness. Human striving cannot outrun guilt or merit mercy. If righteousness depends on our willing or running, grace is abolished and boasting returns. Paul will not permit that. This part of the argument is therefore an assault on spiritual self-confidence. Some people trust their intentions: they mean well, desire God, hope to do better, and assume those desires give them a claim before Him. Others trust their effort: they are active, disciplined, zealous, quick to labor, and imagine that such running secures favor. Paul places both under judgment. Neither willing nor running generates the mercy by which sinners live.

Romans 9:30-10:4

Stumbling Over Christ

31But Israel, which followed after the law of righteousness, hath not attained to the law of righteousness. 32Wherefore? Because they sought it not by faith, but as it were by the works of the law. For they stumbled at that stumblingstone;

Righteousness Near

04 · Stumbling Over Christ

The nations receive righteousness by faith while Israel stumbles through pursuing the law as a ground of achievement rather than coming to Christ.

Paul asks and answers the question directly: "Wherefore? Because they sought it not by faith, but as it were by the works of the law. For they stumbled at that stumblingstone." Here the issue becomes unmistakable. Israel's failure was not caused by lack of law, lack of zeal, or lack of privilege. It was caused by the manner of seeking. They did not seek righteousness by faith. Instead, they approached it as though it could be secured by works. That little phrase "as it were" is revealing. Paul knows the law was never meant to be a means of self-justification, yet that is how it came to function in practice for many. The problem is not obedience itself. Genuine faith bears fruit in obedience, and God's moral law remains holy and good. The problem is using obedience as the ground of acceptance with God. Once righteousness is sought on that basis, the sinner is thrown back upon self, and Christ becomes an offense. That leads directly to the second half of this line: "they stumbled." The failure is not merely intellectual. It is moral and spiritual. They encountered the stone God laid and tripped over it because it contradicted the whole structure of self-righteous pursuit. Christ crucified, Christ as God's righteousness for sinners, Christ received by faith - this humbles the religious heart.

Romans 10:5-21

The Word of Faith Is Near

8But what saith it? The word is nigh thee, even in thy mouth, and in thy heart: that is, the word of faith, which we preach; 9That if thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus, and shalt believe in thine heart that God hath raised him from the dead, thou shalt be saved.

Righteousness Near

05 · The Word of Faith Is Near

The gospel does not demand an impossible ascent. Christ has come and risen; the near word calls for heart-faith, confession, hearing, and mission.

Paul states the content of the saving response with remarkable clarity: "If thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus, and shalt believe in thine heart that God hath raised him from the dead, thou shalt be saved." This is not a magical formula, nor a bare recital of orthodox words. It is the shape of faith itself. To confess Jesus as Lord is public allegiance to Him. To believe in the heart that God raised Him from the dead is inward trust in God's decisive act in Christ. Together they describe a faith that receives Christ personally and openly. The confession "Jesus is Lord" carries enormous weight. It is not merely a statement of respect. It acknowledges His authority, His identity, and His claim upon the believer's life. To confess Him as Lord means that He is not an advisor added to one's existing plans. He is the rightful Master, the risen King, the One to whom one belongs. Such a confession naturally challenges every rival loyalty, whether moral self-rule, religious pride, or worldly power. The gospel does not offer a helper for self-directed lives; it calls sinners under the lordship of Christ. Belief in the resurrection is equally central. Paul does not isolate the resurrection from the cross.

Romans 11:1-10

God Has Not Cast Away His People

1I say then, Hath God cast away his people? God forbid. For I also am an Israelite, of the seed of Abraham, of the tribe of Benjamin. 5Even so then at this present time also there is a remnant according to the election of grace.

A Remnant and a Welcome

06 · God Has Not Cast Away His People

Paul himself and the remnant testify that rejection is neither total nor final. Grace preserves a people without leaving room for works as merit.

Paul begins with a question charged with pain and theological weight: "Has God cast away His people?" The form of the question matters because it grows out of everything he has just said about Israel's unbelief and the inclusion of the Gentiles. Someone could easily conclude that Israel's widespread rejection of Christ means God has abandoned the Jewish people as a whole and broken faith with the covenant story. Paul answers with the strongest refusal he can give. God has not cast away His people in any absolute or final sense. His own life stands as living evidence. He is "an Israelite, of the seed of Abraham, of the tribe of Benjamin." He does not speak as an outsider analyzing Israel from a distance, but as one who belongs to the people whose anguish he has already described. That personal note is not incidental. Paul is saying, in effect, that the existence of a believing Jew like himself shows that God's mercy is still at work among Israel. The gospel has not erased God's faithfulness. It has revealed it. Israel's problem is not that God has suddenly become untrue to His promises. The problem is unbelief, and unbelief is serious. Yet unbelief, even when widespread, does not prove divine rejection in the ultimate sense. God's covenant dealings are not cancelled by human unfaithfulness.

Romans 11:11-16

Salvation Comes to the Nations

11I say then, Have they stumbled that they should fall? God forbid: but rather through their fall salvation is come unto the Gentiles, for to provoke them to jealousy. 12Now if the fall of them be the riches of the world, and the diminishing of them the riches of the Gentiles; how much more their fulness?

A Remnant and a Welcome

07 · Salvation Comes to the Nations

Israel's stumbling becomes an occasion for Gentile salvation, yet Gentile inclusion is meant to awaken hope rather than celebrate another's fall.

Paul asks another decisive question: "Have they stumbled that they should fall?" His answer is immediate: "God forbid." Israel's stumble is real, but it is not presented as the final end of God's purpose for them. Paul refuses the idea that Jewish unbelief means irreversible ruin for all. The distinction between stumbling and falling completely matters. Stumbling can wound, disorient, and bring serious consequences, but it does not have to be the last word. That is why the whole tone of the chapter remains hopeful even while it is honest about unbelief. Paul then explains that through Israel's stumble, salvation has come to the Gentiles "for to provoke them to jealousy." This is not petty jealousy. It is a holy provocation. God's mercy to the nations is meant to awaken Israel to the realization that the blessings of the covenant are being enjoyed by those once far off. Gentile believers, living by faith in Israel's Messiah, are meant to become a witness that stirs longing, not contempt. That is why Gentile arrogance is so out of place in this chapter. If the purpose of Gentile salvation includes awakening Israel, then Gentiles must not behave as though Israel's loss is their occasion for pride. Paul also reveals the astonishing wisdom of God. He does not approve unbelief, and He never makes evil good.

Romans 11:17-24

Do Not Boast Against the Branches

19Thou wilt say then, The branches were broken off, that I might be graffed in. 20Well; because of unbelief they were broken off, and thou standest by faith. Be not highminded, but fear:

One Olive Tree

08 · Do Not Boast Against the Branches

Gentile believers are grafted into a covenant root they did not create. They stand by faith and must exchange superiority for reverent humility.

Paul answers the boast with careful clarity: "Well; because of unbelief they were broken off, and thou standest by faith. Be not high-minded, but fear." He neither denies the historical fact nor allows its misuse. Yes, branches were broken off. But why? Because of unbelief. And why do Gentile believers stand? By faith. That contrast destroys pride at once. Unbelief excludes; faith receives. Neither side gives room for boasting. The one broken off cannot blame God while cherishing unbelief. The one standing cannot boast, because standing itself is by faith - dependence, not achievement. This is one of the most important balancing verses in the chapter. Paul does not flatten Israel's unbelief into insignificance, nor does he flatter Gentile believers into complacency. Unbelief is a serious matter. It has real consequences. But faith, which receives grace, is the opposite of pride. A believer stands by trusting Another, not by securing his own position. "Be not high-minded, but fear" must be read as reverent humility, not servile panic. Paul is not calling believers back into anxious self-salvation. He is warning against presumption. Fear here is the holy seriousness that knows grace is never a reason for carelessness. It is the opposite of the spirit that treats inclusion as entitlement.

Romans 11:25-32

The Mystery of Mercy

30For as ye in times past have not believed God, yet have now obtained mercy through their unbelief: 32For God hath concluded them all in unbelief, that he might have mercy upon all.

One Olive Tree

09 · The Mystery of Mercy

Partial hardening, the fullness of the nations, and Israel's hope all serve a conclusion in which every group depends on divine mercy.

"For God hath concluded them all in unbelief, that he might have mercy upon all." This is one of the chapter's grand summary statements. Paul is not teaching that God caused every act of unbelief in a crude sense, nor that all without exception are automatically saved. The first half of the statement means that God has shut both Jews and Gentiles alike into the same moral reality: all stand exposed as disobedient, all are under sin apart from grace, all are shown to be unable to save themselves. This is consistent with the whole letter. Romans began by indicting the Gentile world and then the religious world, finally placing every mouth under judgment. Here Paul gathers that truth again in a more concentrated form. Why has God done this? "That he might have mercy upon all." The purpose is not to glorify disobedience but to silence boasting and magnify mercy. If Jews could claim privilege and Gentiles could claim superiority, then mercy would be obscured. By exposing the universal condition of unbelief, God brings all to the same place of need. No one is saved by heritage, works, zeal, or knowledge. All who are saved are saved by mercy. The "all" in the second half must be read in harmony with the rest of Romans. Paul is not teaching universalism.

Romans 11:33-36

The Depth of the Wisdom of God

33O the depth of the riches both of the wisdom and knowledge of God! how unsearchable are his judgments, and his ways past finding out! 36For of him, and through him, and to him, are all things: to whom be glory for ever. Amen.

One Olive Tree

10 · The Depth of the Wisdom of God

Paul ends the argument in worship. God is the source, sustainer, and goal of the saving history that human pride cannot master.

Paul can no longer speak of these things without worship: "O the depth of the riches both of the wisdom and knowledge of God!" After tracing Israel's stumbling, Gentile inclusion, the remnant, hardening, mercy, restoration, and the magnitude of divine faithfulness, he does not end in cold system but in adoration. The depth he celebrates is not abstract complexity for its own sake. It is the richness of God's wise and knowing mercy. God has not failed His people; He has not surrendered righteousness; He has not excused unbelief; and yet He has woven mercy through all of it in ways no human planner could have designed. Paul continues: "How unsearchable are his judgments, and his ways past finding out!" That does not mean God is irrational or unknowable in every sense. Paul has spent many chapters letting us know a great deal about God's ways. But after all faithful explanation has been given, there is still depth beyond human mastery. God's judgments are perfectly righteous, yet too deep for creatures to map exhaustively. His ways are revealed enough to produce faith and obedience, yet they surpass human calculation. Paul's words therefore correct two opposite tendencies. One is proud rationalism that thinks it can domesticate God and reduce His work to a scheme under human control.
