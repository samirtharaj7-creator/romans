The Gospel in Romans · Romans 7-8

Law, Flesh, and Spirit

Romans 7-8 distinguishes what is holy from what is powerless. God's law names sin truthfully, yet liberation comes only through Jesus Christ and the indwelling Spirit who forms the life the law describes.

Romans 7:1-6

Freed to Bear Fruit for God

4Wherefore, my brethren, ye also are become dead to the law by the body of Christ; that ye should be married to another, even to him who is raised from the dead, that we should bring forth fruit unto God. 6But now we are delivered from the law, that being dead wherein we were held; that we should serve in newness of spirit, and not in the oldness of the letter.

The Law and the Old Life

01 · Freed to Bear Fruit for God

Believers die to the old realm through Christ so they may belong to the risen Lord and serve in the newness of the Spirit.

Now Paul applies the illustration. "Wherefore, my brethren, ye also are become dead to the law by the body of Christ." This part of the argument is one of the most important in the chapter because it says both what believers are released from and what they are released for. Through the body of Christ - that is, through His death in the flesh - believers die to the law in its former relation to them. Paul is not teaching that God's moral law has become sinful, obsolete, or worthless. He is teaching that those united to Christ no longer stand before the law as condemned persons whose old life remains intact. The death of Christ has changed their position. The law is no longer their jailer or executioner. It is not abolished, but its old claim over them as guilty sinners under sentence is broken. Yet Paul does not stop with release. He immediately says that this death opens the way for a new belonging: "that ye should be married to another, even to him who is raised from the dead." Christianity is not merely escape from guilt. It is union with the risen Christ. The believer is not set free into vacancy, but into fellowship, allegiance, and communion. This is why grace never leaves a person morally neutral.

Romans 7:7-13

The Law Reveals Sin

7What shall we say then? Is the law sin? God forbid. Nay, I had not known sin, but by the law: for I had not known lust, except the law had said, Thou shalt not covet. 12Wherefore the law is holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good.

The Law and the Old Life

02 · The Law Reveals Sin

The commandment is holy and good. Sin exploits the commandment, exposes covetous desire, and turns what is good into an occasion for death.

Paul anticipates the objection his teaching could provoke: "What shall we say then? Is the law sin?" If the law aroused sinful passions and if believers are delivered from the law in the old way, then is the law itself the problem? Paul's answer is immediate and emphatic: "God forbid." He refuses the thought. The law is not sin. It does not belong on the side of evil. Yet he immediately adds that without the law he would not have known sin. The law reveals what sin is. It defines what the sinner might excuse, rename, or hide. In Paul's example, he would not have known coveting except that the law said, "Thou shalt not covet." That choice of example is significant. Coveting reaches inward. This is not merely a prohibition against visible wrongdoing but a commandment that exposes desire. A person may flatter himself that he is decent because he has not committed public scandal, and yet the law enters the secret life and lays hold of envy, craving, possessiveness, and self-pleasing desire. The commandment thus destroys shallow views of goodness. It shows that sin is not limited to external acts. It begins in the heart's disordered wants. Paul is not saying that people outside the written law have no awareness of evil whatsoever.

Romans 7:14-23

The Divided Struggle Under Sin

18For I know that in me (that is, in my flesh,) dwelleth no good thing: for to will is present with me; but how to perform that which is good I find not. 19For the good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do.

The Captive Self

03 · The Divided Struggle Under Sin

Knowledge and desire cannot free the captive will. Paul portrays the anguish of recognizing the good while another power works in the members.

Paul repeats the contradiction in slightly fuller form. Repetition here is not redundancy. It is emphasis born of anguish. The problem is not occasional inconsistency but a settled pattern of impotence. The person before us genuinely values the good in some measure, yet does not carry it out. Yet what he rejects at the level of conscience becomes actual conduct. The repetition itself communicates frustration: the circle keeps closing, the same defeat keeps appearing, the will keeps collapsing under the weight of indwelling sin. The statement is one reason Romans 7 speaks so forcefully to the human condition. It exposes the limits of moral aspiration. There is a world of difference between admiring goodness and becoming good. The flesh can appreciate righteousness, even long for relief from evil, and still produce evil. That destroys every optimistic theology of self-improvement. If sin could be overcome by clarity of mind or intensity of will, Paul would not speak like this. Paul's sentence lays bare the tragic gap between moral desire and moral power. Even so, it would be wrong to use this part of the argument as permission for spiritual defeat. Paul is not teaching believers to shrug and say, "This is just how life is." He is writing toward a cry for deliverance.

Romans 7:24-25

Who Shall Deliver Me?

24O wretched man that I am! who shall deliver me from the body of this death? 25I thank God through Jesus Christ our Lord. So then with the mind I myself serve the law of God; but with the flesh the law of sin.

The Captive Self

04 · Who Shall Deliver Me?

The chapter's cry does not end in introspection. Thanksgiving names Jesus Christ as the only deliverer from the body of death.

The chapter reaches its emotional climax in the cry for deliverance. This is not mild discouragement. It is desperate honesty. The person Paul has described can no longer pretend that knowledge, desire, or law-keeping in human strength will save him. He sees his misery and names it. Wretched means afflicted, exhausted, distressed by the condition already detailed. He is not merely disappointed with himself. He recognizes that he is trapped in a realm of sin and death from which he cannot extract himself. The question is equally important: "Who shall deliver me?" Not "what technique," not "what rule," not "what renewed effort," but "who." The chapter has done its work. It has driven the reader away from self and even away from law as a means of rescue. The answer must come from a Person. Deliverance is not improvement but liberation. The cry assumes helplessness and therefore becomes the threshold of grace. So long as a person still asks how he can fix himself, he has not yet reached the depth of Romans 7. When he asks who will deliver, he is near the gospel. "The body of this death" should not be turned into contempt for embodiment itself. Paul is speaking of the whole self as bound up in mortality, corruption, and sin's deadly regime.

Romans 8:1-4

No Condemnation in Christ

1There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus, who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit. 2For the law of the Spirit of life in Christ Jesus hath made me free from the law of sin and death.

Life in the Spirit

05 · No Condemnation in Christ

The Spirit's law of life frees those in Christ from sin and death. God acts in His Son so the law's righteous requirement may be fulfilled.

Romans 8 opens by gathering up the ache of chapter 7 and answering it with the gospel: there is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus. Paul has just described the misery of a life that knows the good yet cannot produce it by its own power. He does not answer that misery by lowering God's standard, excusing sin, or treating settled bondage as the believer's final home. He answers it with Christ Himself. The word therefore matters. Paul is drawing a conclusion from the saving work he has already unfolded: believers have died with Christ, have been joined to Him, and receive from Him what the law could never supply. No condemnation is not a mood, not self-forgiveness, and not a religious pep talk. It is God's verdict in favor of those who are in His Son. The sentence that stood against the sinner has been answered by the death and resurrection of Jesus. Yet this freedom from condemnation must not be turned into indifference about holiness. Paul speaks in KJV language of those who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit, and the chapter itself explains that being in Christ never means being left under sin's old mastery. Grace frees from guilt, but it also opens the way to a new manner of life.

Romans 8:5-13

The Mind of the Spirit

5For they that are after the flesh do mind the things of the flesh; but they that are after the Spirit the things of the Spirit. 6For to be carnally minded is death; but to be spiritually minded is life and peace.

Life in the Spirit

06 · The Mind of the Spirit

Flesh and Spirit describe opposed orientations. The indwelling Spirit gives life and empowers believers to put sinful deeds to death.

Paul presses the contrast further: "For to be carnally minded is death; but to be spiritually minded is life and peace." He is not speaking merely of future outcomes, though eternal consequences are certainly included. He is describing present realities already at work. The mind set on the flesh participates now in death because it is alienated from God, the source of life. It may appear energetic, successful, cultured, even religious, but underneath there is estrangement, disintegration, and the shadow of judgment. Death in Paul's thought is more than the ending of biological life. It is existence severed from God, and therefore moving toward corruption and ruin. By contrast, the mind set on the Spirit is "life and peace." Life means more than continued existence. It is participation in the life God gives in Christ, the beginning of resurrection power within the present age. Peace is not mere inward calm or emotional ease. It is reconciliation with God and the inward wholeness that grows out of that reconciliation. Peace includes a new relation to God, a new relation to oneself, and the beginning of rightly ordered relations with others. Where the Spirit rules, the person is no longer at war with God's will. That does not remove struggle, sorrow, or suffering, but it does change the basic condition of the heart.

Romans 8:14-17

Adoption and the Cry of Abba

14For as many as are led by the Spirit of God, they are the sons of God. 15For ye have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear; but ye have received the Spirit of adoption, whereby we cry, Abba, Father.

Children and Heirs

07 · Adoption and the Cry of Abba

Those led by the Spirit are not returned to fear. They receive adoption, cry to the Father, and share Christ's inheritance and path.

Paul contrasts two conditions. The "spirit of bondage again to fear" recalls life under condemnation, slavery, and anxious distance from God. The gospel does not return believers to that old servile terror. It does not produce a religion in which people try to survive before God by performance while always expecting rejection. That kind of fear belongs to slavery, not sonship. In its place believers have received "the Spirit of adoption." Adoption is not a lesser form of belonging. It is God's gracious act of bringing those who were outside His family into the full standing of sons and heirs. The Spirit does not merely tell believers that such a thing is possible. He brings them into its experience, so that they cry, "Abba, Father." This cry is intimate, but not casual. It is the language of real closeness joined with reverence. God is no longer a distant principle, nor only a judge beyond reach. He is the Father to whom the heart turns in dependence, trust, and love. The word "cry" matters. Paul is not describing cool formality. This is not merely a correct theory about God. The Spirit awakens a filial address that rises from need, confidence, and affection. That is why the statement gives such help to troubled consciences. Assurance is not built on pretending that God owes sinners a welcome.

Romans 8:18-27

Creation and the Spirit Groan

22For we know that the whole creation groaneth and travaileth in pain together until now. 23And not only they, but ourselves also, which have the firstfruits of the Spirit, even we ourselves groan within ourselves, waiting for the adoption, to wit, the redemption of our body.

Children and Heirs

08 · Creation and the Spirit Groan

Present suffering belongs to a creation awaiting liberation. Hope waits, and the Spirit intercedes within weakness according to God's will.

Paul gives the common Christian knowledge of the matter: creation groans. This is not merely poetic flourish. He is describing the universal experience of a world subject to corruption. The groaning is comprehensive - "the whole creation." The fall has left no corner untouched. The created order bears the mark of pain from one end to the other. But the image Paul chooses is important: not the groan of meaningless collapse only, but the travail of childbirth. Childbirth imagery includes pain, but it also points forward. Something is coming. The suffering is not final emptiness but labor toward new emergence. Paul therefore does not romanticize the pain of the world, yet neither does he read it as hopeless. Creation's groaning is intense, continuous, and shared, but it is tied to the hope of birth. That fits the chapter's whole movement from suffering to glory, from futility to freedom, from groaning to redemption. Paul's sentence helps believers look at the natural world without sentimentality and without despair. Paul does not idealize creation as though it were currently in perfect harmony. He sees the pain clearly - decay, loss, disorder, death. Nor does he permit a cynical reading of existence in which suffering empties the world of meaning. The groans are real, but they occur within a story God is bringing to fulfillment.

Romans 8:28-30

Conformed to the Image of the Son

28And we know that all things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are the called according to his purpose. 29For whom he did foreknow, he also did predestinate to be conformed to the image of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brethren.

Purpose and Assurance

09 · Conformed to the Image of the Son

God's purpose is not merely relief from difficulty but a people shaped into Christ's likeness and brought toward final glory.

Paul now unfolds the divine purpose behind the good of verse 28. The destination is not merely relief from trouble, but conformity to Christ. That is the good God is working toward. He means to shape His people into the likeness of His Son. The gospel is therefore not only rescue from penalty; it is restoration into Christlikeness. The whole chapter points that way - Spirit-led life now, glory later, and Jesus Himself as the pattern of both suffering and exaltation. The language of foreknowing and predestining must be handled with reverence and balance. Paul's aim here is not to encourage fatalism, resentment, or curiosity about hidden decrees. He writes to assure believers that their salvation stands within God's prior purpose. In the flow of Romans, foreknowledge is more than bare awareness; it carries the sense of God's prior regard, purpose, and relation toward those who are His. Predestination here is not a dark doctrine of arbitrariness, but the setting of a gracious destiny. God's purpose for His people is that they become like His Son. This guards Christian assurance from shallowness. If God's purpose were only to forgive, one might imagine salvation ending in moral unchangedness. Paul says God's purpose includes transformation. If God's purpose were only transformation by human striving, assurance would collapse under the weight of self.

Romans 8:31-39

Nothing Can Separate Us

38For I am persuaded, that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers, nor things present, nor things to come, 39Nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord.

Purpose and Assurance

10 · Nothing Can Separate Us

The chapter closes with confidence grounded in God's giving of His Son. No created power can sever believers from His love in Christ.

Paul's assurance now becomes personal and expansive. "I am persuaded" is not a fragile opinion. It is settled conviction born of the gospel he has expounded. He then begins gathering every conceivable threat into a sweeping list. Death and life - both the final enemy and the entire sphere of earthly existence - cannot separate believers from God's love in Christ. Neither the worst that ends life nor the complexities of life itself can do it. He adds spiritual powers: angels, principalities, powers. Paul does not pause here to indulge curiosity about the invisible world. His point is simpler and stronger. No created spiritual agency, however formidable, can outmatch the love of God in Christ. He then includes "things present" and "things to come." The burdens of now and the fears of what may yet arrive are both named and both put beneath the sovereignty of divine love. This is immensely practical because many believers are tormented as much by anticipation as by reality. They may accept that God held them yesterday and still fear what tomorrow might bring. Paul says the future itself cannot sever them from Christ. That includes events they cannot predict, powers they cannot see, and changes they cannot control. The whole region of uncertainty is covered by the same assurance that covers present troubles.
