Righteousness by Faith · Article 05

The Just Shall Live by Faith: The Heart of Romans

Romans 1:16-17 unlocks the whole letter: God reveals the righteousness sinners need, faith receives what grace provides, and the believer continues to live in dependence on Christ.

Romans 1:16-17 Faith 14 min read

The Heartbeat of Romans

Romans opens with a declaration that has shaped Christian faith for centuries: "The just shall live by faith." Paul does not place those words in the letter as a passing motto or a memorable line to decorate the introduction. He places them at the front because they unlock everything that follows. If a reader grasps what Paul means there, the whole letter begins to open. If that statement is misunderstood, Romans will be misunderstood at nearly every major turn.

Many people come to Romans looking for answers about justification, the law, grace, assurance, Israel, obedience, or the struggle with sin. All of those questions matter, and Paul addresses them with extraordinary depth. Yet he begins in a simpler and more foundational place: the gospel reveals the righteousness of God, and the righteous live by faith. That is not a side issue. It is the center of the letter's message and movement.

The force of that opening claim becomes even clearer when one notices how Romans unfolds. Paul first shows the ruin of guilty humanity. Jew and Gentile alike stand under sin. Human beings do not produce the righteousness they need, and they cannot rescue themselves by privilege, religious knowledge, or moral effort. Into that helpless condition God reveals His righteousness in the gospel of His Son. Sinners are justified freely by grace through Christ. The result is not careless living, but peace with God, new life, and a walk shaped by trust, surrender, and hope. Faith, then, is not only the hand that first receives salvation. It is the ongoing posture of the believer before God.

The words "the just shall live by faith" stand against several common errors at once. They oppose religious pride, because faith excludes boasting. They oppose unbelief, because God's saving righteousness is received, not manufactured. They oppose lawlessness, because the life that springs from faith is a life reconciled to God and led into obedience. They oppose despair, because the righteousness needed for life comes from God, not from the fluctuating strength of the sinner. Paul does not call people to admire righteousness from a distance. He announces that in the gospel God gives what He commands, and faith receives what grace provides.

Why Romans 1:16-17 Is the Key Text

Paul says he is not ashamed of the gospel because it is the power of God unto salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek. That sentence already gathers several themes that will run through the whole letter. The gospel is God's saving action, not human moral achievement. Salvation is offered without ethnic restriction. Believing is central. Then, in the next verse, Paul explains why the gospel is so powerful: in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith to faith, as it is written, "The just shall live by faith."

  1. What the gospel reveals.It reveals God's own righteous saving action in Christ, not a standard sinners must meet by themselves.
  2. How salvation is received.It is to everyone who believes. Faith is the appointed way sinners receive God's saving provision.
  3. Who the gospel reaches.It is for the Jew first and also for the Greek, standing at the center of God's saving purpose for humanity.
  4. What kind of life it creates.The righteous live by faith. Continued dependence on God is the atmosphere of the believer's life.

When these verses are treated merely as an introduction, readers often lose the thread of Romans. But when they are taken as the doorway to the whole letter, the flow becomes clearer. Romans moves from humanity's lack of righteousness to God's gift of righteousness in Christ, and then to the life that grows out of that gift.

The Righteousness Revealed in the Gospel

The phrase "the righteousness of God" has great weight in Romans. Paul returns to it at the major turning point of the letter in Romans 3:21-26. After exposing the sin of the Gentile world, the hypocrisy of the moralist, and the guilt of those who possess the law yet fail to keep it, Paul brings the whole world under judgment. Every mouth is stopped. No one can boast. By works of law no flesh will be justified in God's sight, because the law gives the knowledge of sin. That is the dark backdrop against which Romans 1:16-17 shines even more brightly.

Then comes one of the great "now" statements of Scripture: "But now the righteousness of God apart from law is revealed." Paul is not contradicting the law in the sense of setting God's revealed will aside. He is saying that righteousness as a means of justification does not come by human law-keeping. It comes through Jesus Christ to all and upon all who believe. The problem is universal: all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God. The provision is gracious: sinners are justified freely by His grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.

That is why Romans 1:17 is the heart of the letter. It anticipates this entire saving movement. The righteousness revealed in the gospel is the answer to the universal failure exposed in Romans 1-3. Human beings do not climb up to God through their own performance. God reveals His righteousness in Christ and gives it to those who believe.

Paul also insists that this way of salvation excludes boasting. If sinners are justified by grace through faith, there is no room for self-congratulation. Faith does not allow the sinner to say, "I have earned God's acceptance." It says, "I have received what I could never deserve." The gospel humbles before it comforts. It strips away claims of merit before it gives peace.

This is one reason Romans cannot be read as if faith were a human achievement. Paul is at pains to show that salvation leaves no place for glorying in self. A faith that turns into self-admiration has already ceased to be biblical faith. True faith looks away from self to Christ. It receives, rests, trusts, and clings.

At the same time, Paul refuses the idea that faith is morally empty. He explicitly asks whether faith makes the law void and answers, "God forbid: yea, we establish the law." The gospel does not save by human obedience, but neither does it produce contempt for God's will. The righteousness received by faith restores the sinner to God and opens the way for a life no longer ruled by rebellion and self-justification.

What Faith Is and Is Not

Because faith stands at the center of Romans, it is easy for people to misuse the word. Paul's argument helps protect us from several distortions.

Not mere agreement

Faith is personal reliance upon God's promise in Christ. It is the surrender of self-confidence and the turning of the sinner from his own righteousness toward the righteousness God provides.

Not a substitute for transformation

Justification in Romans 3-5 leads into union with Christ in Romans 6, struggle honestly faced in Romans 7, and life in the Spirit in Romans 8. Faith makes holiness possible in its proper place.

Not a work that earns favor

Faith is counted for righteousness because it does not present achievement to God as a claim. Works seek wages as something owed; faith receives righteousness as a gift of grace.

Not only the beginning

The righteous live by faith. Peace, access into grace, standing firm, hope, and Christian obedience all grow from continued dependence upon Christ.

This kind of faith produces humility. It also produces peace. Where faith looks to Christ instead of self, the anxious effort to justify oneself begins to die. That does not create passivity. It creates steady, grateful obedience. The person living by faith is no longer trying to force God's acceptance through performance. He serves as one already received in Christ.

Abraham as the Pattern of Faith

Paul turns to Abraham in Romans 4 because he wants to show what living by faith looks like in concrete, biblical form. Abraham is not presented as a religious achiever who secured divine favor through his own excellence. He is presented as one who believed God. His faith did not create the promise. It received it. His righteousness was not the reward for flawless performance. It was counted in relation to faith.

That matters enormously for the argument of Romans. Abraham stands before both Jewish and Gentile readers as a witness that the way of righteousness has always been the way of faith. Paul is not inventing a new gospel detached from earlier Scripture. He is showing the consistency of God's saving way. Abraham believed before he could boast in outward distinctions as a means of righteousness. He became the father of all who believe.

Romans 4 also shows that faith deals honestly with impossibility. Abraham considered the deadness of his own body and the barrenness of Sarah's womb, yet he did not stagger at the promise of God through unbelief. He was strengthened in faith, giving glory to God, being fully persuaded that what God had promised, He was able also to perform. That is important because biblical faith is not optimism about human potential. It is confidence in God's faithfulness where human strength fails.

In that sense, Abraham is not merely an example of how one enters salvation; he is a pattern of the whole life of faith. He trusted where sight offered no encouragement. He relied on promise rather than visible capacity. He gave glory to God by believing that God would do what He said.

Paul then brings the pattern directly to believers in Christ. The words about righteousness being counted were not written for Abraham's sake alone, but for ours also. Those who believe in Him who raised up Jesus our Lord from the dead stand in that same line of faith. The God who justified Abraham by promise is the God who has acted decisively in Christ's death and resurrection for our justification.

Faith, then, is not vague spirituality. It has content. It believes God's saving promise centered in Jesus Christ. It receives the crucified and risen Lord as the ground of righteousness and peace.

Living by Faith in the Christian Life

Romans 5:1-2 shows the immediate fruit of the faith Paul has been describing: "Therefore being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ: by whom also we have access by faith into this grace wherein we stand." Those words make clear that faith is not a thin doctrine for theological debate. It is the living way by which a sinner enters peace, stands in grace, and rejoices in hope.

Peace with God does not mean that all struggle disappears from the believer's life. Romans will go on to speak of conflict with sin, groaning creation, weakness, suffering, and the need for endurance. But peace with God means that the deepest war has ended. The believer is no longer under condemnation, trying to settle the question of acceptance by personal merit. In Christ, reconciliation has been accomplished.

Access into grace also means that faith is ongoing. Paul does not picture grace as a doorway a believer walks through once and then leaves behind. Grace becomes the sphere in which the believer stands. Faith remains the posture of standing there. The Christian life is not sustained by shifting from Christ to self. It continues by abiding in the same grace that first justified.

This protects against two destructive habits. The first is despair. When believers look mainly at their failures, their weakness, or the unevenness of their growth, they may conclude that life with God rests on their own consistency. Romans points them back to Christ. The righteous live by faith. The stability of the Christian life is not found in human strength but in the righteousness revealed in the gospel. The second habit is pride. When people begin to think they now stand by spiritual performance, they forget how they began. Faith excludes boasting at the beginning, and it continues to exclude boasting as the Christian life unfolds.

Yet living by faith does not produce indifference toward obedience. Romans as a whole will not allow that. Paul moves from justification to a life no longer under the dominion of sin. He moves from peace with God to yielding ourselves unto God. He moves from grace to transformed living. If obedience is severed from faith, it becomes legal striving. If faith is severed from obedience, it becomes empty profession. In Romans, faith works differently. It receives Christ, unites the sinner to Him, brings peace, destroys boasting, and begins a new life under grace.

So the believer who lives by faith does not ask, "How little obedience can I offer while still claiming grace?" He asks, "How shall I, who have received such mercy, live unto God?" Faith changes the center of life. It no longer treats the self as savior. It no longer treats performance as the ground of acceptance. It no longer treats sin as harmless. It places Christ at the center and learns to walk from Him, through Him, and for Him.

Why This Truth Shapes the Whole Letter

When Paul says the righteous shall live by faith, he is giving more than a slogan. He is giving the interpretive center of Romans. The universal darkness of human guilt makes sense in light of the righteousness revealed in the gospel. Justification by grace makes sense in light of faith receiving what it cannot earn. Abraham makes sense as the father of believers because he trusted promise rather than works. Peace with God makes sense because faith lays hold of Christ. The later calls to holiness, humility, unity, and love make sense because the entire Christian life grows out of the mercies of God received by faith.

That is why faith cannot be treated as a small doctrinal detail in Romans. It is woven into the whole letter. It answers guilt without feeding pride. It establishes righteousness without making salvation a human achievement. It produces peace without creating carelessness. It opens the door equally to Jew and Gentile. It allows God alone to receive the glory.

Romans begins with the gospel as the power of God unto salvation, and it ends with glory to the only wise God through Jesus Christ forever. Faith belongs to that whole movement. It is the empty hand receiving grace, the humble heart renouncing boasting, and the living bond by which the believer continues in Christ. Remove that center, and Romans collapses into either moralism or confusion. Keep it in place, and the letter holds together in all its force.

The righteous shall live by faith. That is where Romans begins, and it is the heartbeat that can still be heard in every major movement of the book.
