Peace and Assurance · Article 11

Peace With God Through Jesus Christ

Romans 5 shows what justification gives: peace with God, access into grace, hope that survives suffering, and reconciliation grounded in Christ's love.

Romans 5:1-11 Peace and Reconciliation 15 min read

Introduction

Romans 5 opens like sunlight after storm. The earlier chapters have already done hard work. They have exposed the guilt of the world, stripped away human boasting, and declared that sinners are justified by faith apart from works of law. Then Paul turns to the blessings that flow from that verdict. He does not leave justification as a courtroom term floating above daily life. He shows what it gives. Peace with God. Access into grace. Hope of glory. Endurance in suffering. Assurance rooted in divine love. Reconciliation through the death of Christ.

That movement matters. Many believers know that forgiveness is possible, but they are not sure what changes once a person is justified. Others speak of peace as though it were mainly a mood, something that rises and falls with a good day or a quiet mind. Romans 5 speaks more deeply. The peace Paul describes is not a passing sense of calm. It is a new standing before God through Jesus Christ. The believer is no longer under condemnation, no longer alienated, no longer facing God as Judge only, but brought near through the saving work of His Son.

This passage also protects readers from shallow accounts of assurance. Paul does not pretend that justified believers will escape suffering. He says the opposite. Suffering remains part of the Christian path. Yet it no longer speaks the final word. It becomes the setting in which endurance, tested character, and hope are deepened. The confidence of Romans 5 is not built on easy circumstances. It stands on Christ's death for the ungodly, God's love poured into the heart, and the certainty that the God who reconciled enemies to Himself will finish what He began.

The Passage in Context

Romans 5:1-11 belongs to a larger argument. From Romans 1:18 onward, Paul has shown the ruin of human sin. Gentiles stand guilty. Jews also stand guilty. Religious privilege cannot remove guilt. Moral effort cannot silence accusation. The law exposes sin, but it cannot justify the sinner. By the time Paul reaches the end of Romans 3, every mouth is stopped and the whole world stands accountable before God.

Then comes the great turning point. God has revealed His righteousness in Christ apart from works of law as a means of justification. Sinners are justified by grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus. Faith receives what grace provides. Boasting is excluded. Abraham becomes the great example of righteousness counted through faith, not earned by achievement.

Romans 5 begins with “therefore.” That word matters. Paul is not changing subjects. He is drawing out the consequence of justification. If sinners are justified by faith, what follows? The answer is not merely that they have a future hope of heaven. The answer begins now. They have peace with God. They stand in grace. They rejoice in hope. They are sustained in suffering. They know the love of God. They are reconciled through Christ.

Read this way, Romans 5 becomes the bridge from justification to the wider life of faith. It gathers up what Paul has said about righteousness by faith and begins to show what that righteousness means for daily life, assurance, perseverance, and joy.

From Justification to Peace

“Therefore, being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ.” Paul begins with the strongest possible foundation. Peace with God is the first major blessing he names because everything that follows grows from it.

This peace is not merely inward quietness, though inward quietness may grow from it. It is first a restored relation. Sin had created enmity. Human beings were not merely anxious; they were alienated. The problem was not simply that people felt far from God. They truly were far from Him, standing under judgment. Justification changes that relation. The one who was guilty is now accepted in Christ. The warfare between the sinner and God, created by rebellion and guilt, is brought to an end through Jesus Christ.

The wording also keeps the believer from looking inward for the ground of peace. Paul does not say we have peace through our own reform, spiritual effort, or moral record. The peace comes “through our Lord Jesus Christ.” The entire passage is Christ-centered. Justification is through Him, peace is through Him, access is through Him, reconciliation is through Him, salvation from wrath is through Him, and joy in God is through Him. Romans 5 leaves no room for self-congratulation. What the believer possesses has been given through Another.

That also means peace with God is sturdier than emotion. Feelings shift. Faith may tremble. Trials may unsettle the mind. Yet the peace described here rests on God's act in Christ, not on the believer's emotional consistency. A Christian may at times struggle to feel peace and yet still possess peace with God because the ground of that peace lies outside the self. Paul is describing a new reality established by grace, not a fragile inward sensation maintained by mood.

Access, Grace, and Hope

Paul continues by saying that through Christ “we have access by faith into this grace wherein we stand, and rejoice in hope of the glory of God.” Here he adds two more gifts: access and hope.

Access means more than a brief entrance. It portrays the privilege of being brought near to God. The justified believer is not left at a distance, uncertain whether welcome will continue. He is brought into grace as a sphere of life. Paul does not speak as though believers step into grace for a moment and then must keep themselves there by their own worthiness. He says, “wherein we stand.” This is language of security and position. Grace is not a narrow doorway behind which the believer is instantly left alone. It is the realm in which the redeemed stand before God.

That standing produces hope. The hope of Romans 5 is not wishful thinking. It is confident expectation grounded in God's saving work. Paul lifts the eyes of believers toward “the glory of God.” The justified do not merely look back to pardon; they look forward to final restoration. Salvation is not a bare escape from penalty. It is movement toward sharing in the life and glory God intends for His people.

This hope is one reason Romans will not allow a starving, fearful version of Christianity. Paul does not teach believers to spend their days wondering whether Christ's work is enough. He teaches them to rejoice in hope. That rejoicing is not arrogance. It is gratitude rooted in grace. The person justified by faith does not boast in self but may exult in what God has promised.

Why Suffering Does Not Overthrow Assurance

Paul adds one of the most surprising lines in the passage: “And not only so, but we glory in tribulations also.” He does not deny suffering. He places it inside the life of justification. That is one reason Romans 5 is so pastorally strong. It refuses two common errors. One error says that suffering proves God has abandoned us. The other says that strong faith should shield us from affliction. Paul teaches neither.

Tribulations do not overthrow justification because they do not cancel Christ's work. Instead, under grace, suffering becomes part of the believer's formation. Tribulation works patience, patience produces tested character, and tested character strengthens hope. This is not because suffering is good in itself, nor because pain automatically sanctifies. It is because God is at work in the justified believer, shaping endurance and deepening settled confidence in Him.

Then Paul says hope does not put us to shame because the love of God has been poured out in our hearts through the Holy Spirit. This is a vital line. Christian assurance is not cold deduction alone. It is not less than truth, but it is more than argument. God gives the Spirit so that His love is known inwardly, not as fantasy, but as a real work of grace in the heart. The believer is not left merely to infer divine love from distant doctrines. The Spirit makes that love personally known.

Still, Paul does not leave assurance resting on inward experience. He anchors it in history. The inward testimony of the Spirit is bound to the outward act of Christ. That protects believers from turning assurance into self-analysis. If one's only evidence is inward warmth, fear will eventually return. Paul gives both: the Spirit's witness within and Christ's death for the ungodly in objective history.

Christ Died for the Ungodly

At the center of the passage stands one of the clearest descriptions of grace in all Scripture: “When we were yet without strength, in due time Christ died for the ungodly.” Paul wants the reader to feel the force of the word “ungodly.” Christ did not die for people who had first repaired themselves. He did not wait until they became morally attractive. He died for those who were helpless, sinful, and estranged.

Paul deepens that thought by comparing ordinary human love with the love revealed in Christ. A person might, in rare cases, die for a righteous man or perhaps for a good man. But God demonstrates His love in this: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. The logic is breathtaking. God did not merely say that He loves sinners. He demonstrated it in the death of His Son.

This guards justification from two opposite errors. One error says God ignores sin. Romans 5 will not allow that. Christ had to die. Reconciliation comes through blood, through sacrifice, through a real dealing with guilt. The cross is not proof that sin does not matter; it is proof that sin matters terribly. The other error says divine love must be earned. Romans 5 shatters that delusion as well. Christ died not for the worthy but for the ungodly.

Paul then argues from the greater to the lesser. If God loved us enough to justify us through Christ's blood while we were still sinners, much more shall we be saved from wrath through Him now that we are justified. If reconciliation took place when we were enemies, how much more will those who are reconciled be saved by His life. This is not careless optimism. It is confidence grounded in the logic of grace. The God who has done the greater thing will not fail to do the lesser.

Reconciliation and Joy in God

The climactic word in the passage is reconciliation. Paul says that while we were enemies, we were reconciled to God by the death of His Son. Reconciliation means the end of estrangement and the restoration of fellowship. It carries the passage beyond legal acquittal into relational joy. Justification and reconciliation are not identical ideas, but in Romans 5 they belong together. The justified sinner is not merely declared safe and sent away. He is brought back to God.

That is why Paul ends not with abstract relief but with joy: “we also joy in God through our Lord Jesus Christ, by whom we have now received the reconciliation.” The goal of salvation is not merely escape from wrath, though it includes that. Nor is it merely personal peace, though it includes that as well. The goal is joy in God Himself. Salvation restores the creature to where it belongs—in the delight of fellowship with the One from whom sin had separated it.

This rescues Christian assurance from self-absorption. Some people want peace because they want relief from fear, but they do not yet see that the greatest gift of peace is restored fellowship with God. Reconciliation means God is no longer only the One from whom sinners once hid. In Christ He becomes the One in whom they now rejoice.

Common Misunderstandings

Peace is only emotional serenity

One misunderstanding treats peace with God as though Paul were describing emotional serenity only. That reduces the depth of the passage. Emotional peace matters, but Romans 5 begins with a changed standing before God. Feeling may follow; the relationship comes first.

Suffering disproves grace

Another misunderstanding assumes that suffering disproves grace. Paul writes the opposite. Tribulations do not remove believers from grace; they become part of a hope-shaped life under grace. The Christian does not interpret suffering as evidence that justification has collapsed.

Assurance rests on performance

Another error places assurance in personal performance. Romans 5 directs assurance away from self and toward Christ's death, Christ's blood, Christ's life, and God's demonstrated love. The believer does grow in holiness, but assurance rests fundamentally on the finished work of Christ, not on a flawless spiritual record.

Justification leaves people unchanged

Another error separates justification from transformation altogether. Romans 5 does not do that. It emphasizes peace, access, hope, and reconciliation, but it also points toward a life reshaped by the Spirit, sustained in suffering, and moved to joy in God. Grace does not leave people unchanged. It does not justify in order to excuse spiritual indifference.

Why This Matters for Reading Romans

Romans 5 helps readers understand that the gospel is larger than forgiveness alone while never becoming something other than grace. Paul has argued for justification by faith in Romans 3 and 4. Here he shows its fruit. Later he will speak about freedom from sin's dominion, the struggle with indwelling sin, life in the Spirit, God's faithfulness to His promises, and the transformed life of the church. Romans 5 is one of the places where those later themes begin to open.

Without this chapter, justification could be misunderstood as a bare legal verdict. With this chapter, it becomes clear that justification leads into a life of peace, access, assurance, endurance, love, hope, and reconciliation. It humbles the proud because it is grounded in Christ's death for the ungodly. It comforts the fearful because it anchors assurance in God's demonstrated love. It prepares the way for holy living because the justified are not only pardoned; they are restored to God.

Romans 5 also guards the church from two destructive habits. One is triumphalism that has no place for tears, weakness, or tribulation. The other is anxious religion that never rests in Christ's completed work. Paul rejects both. The justified rejoice and suffer. They hope and endure. They are deeply assured and still very much dependent. They do not deny hardship, but neither do they surrender joy.

Final Reflection

Romans 5 does not ask believers to manufacture peace. It announces peace through Jesus Christ. It does not tell them to force hope out of thin air. It places them in grace and points them toward glory. It does not deny the reality of suffering. It teaches them how to suffer without losing assurance. Above all, it fixes their eyes on Christ, who died not for the strong or worthy, but for the ungodly.

That is why this passage remains so precious. It shows what justification gives: not simply a new status on paper, but a new relation to God, a new standing in grace, a new outlook on suffering, a new confidence in divine love, and a new joy in God Himself. The believer stands where grace has placed him and looks forward where grace is leading him, all through our Lord Jesus Christ.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does “peace with God” mean in Romans 5?

It means a restored relationship with God through Jesus Christ. Paul is speaking first about reconciliation and acceptance before God, not merely about inward calm.

Is peace with God the same as feeling peaceful?

Not exactly. Emotional peace may grow from it, but Romans 5 describes an objective change in standing before God. A believer may struggle emotionally and still truly have peace with God through Christ.

Why does Paul mention suffering so soon after justification?

Because justification does not remove believers from a broken world. Paul shows that suffering does not cancel grace. Under God's hand, tribulation becomes a place where endurance, tested character, and hope are deepened.

How does Romans 5 support assurance?

It anchors assurance in God's demonstrated love in Christ's death for sinners. The believer's confidence rests not in personal worthiness but in what God has already done through His Son.

What is reconciliation in Romans 5?

Reconciliation is the ending of estrangement between sinners and God through the death of Christ. It means believers are not only pardoned but brought back into fellowship, joy, and peace with God.
