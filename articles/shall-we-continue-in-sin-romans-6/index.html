Shall We Continue in Sin? Grace and Victory in Romans 6

Romans 6 answers cheap grace by joining believers to Christ's death and resurrection, breaking sin's dominion, and opening a new life under righteousness.

Romans 6:1-23 Grace and Victory 15 min read

Introduction

Romans 6 stands at one of the most important turning points in the letter. After proclaiming justification by faith and the triumph of grace, Paul faces a question that always appears when the gospel is preached clearly: if salvation is by grace, does obedience still matter? If God forgives freely, can a believer continue in sin without serious concern?

Paul answers that question with a forceful refusal. Grace does not make peace with sin. Grace does not reduce holiness to an optional extra. Grace joins the believer to Christ in such a way that the old relation to sin is broken, a new life begins, and a new Master is embraced.

That is why Romans 6 is not a side issue in Paul's argument. It is the necessary answer to a false conclusion drawn from the good news. At the end of Romans 5, grace is shown to be greater than sin. Where sin abounded, grace abounded much more. But that very declaration can be twisted. If grace rises above sin, why not let sin continue so grace may appear even greater? Romans 6 exists because the gospel can be slandered by those who hear pardon but do not understand union with Christ.

The False Question Grace Provokes

Paul opens the chapter with a question that sounds shocking: “Shall we continue in sin, that grace may abound?” (Rom. 6:1). He does not answer cautiously. He answers decisively: “God forbid” (Rom. 6:2). The strength of the reply matters. Paul is not saying, “That would be unwise,” or, “That might weaken your testimony.” He is saying that such a conclusion contradicts the very nature of the salvation he has been describing.

The question itself shows that Paul has not been preaching a message of human merit. No one asks whether law-keeping encourages sin. That accusation comes only when grace has been proclaimed with full force. But the answer shows that grace has been misunderstood if it is treated as a permission slip for rebellion.

Paul's reasoning begins with identity, not with threats. “How shall we, that are dead to sin, live any longer therein?” (Rom. 6:2). He does not say merely that believers should die to sin. He says something has already happened that changes the whole moral situation. A transfer has taken place. A severing has taken place. Those who belong to Christ cannot think of sin the way they once did, because their relation to it has been altered by Christ Himself.

This sets the tone for the whole chapter. Paul does not ground holy living in self-confidence or religious pride. He grounds it in participation in the saving work of Jesus.

Dead With Christ, Alive With Christ

Paul next turns to baptism, not as a bare ritual, but as a sign of union with Christ: “so many of us as were baptized into Jesus Christ were baptized into his death” (Rom. 6:3). His point is not to discuss every question people may raise about baptism in general. His point is that the believer's life is now tied to the death and resurrection of Christ. Baptism speaks of burial and newness of life because the believer has been brought into a new reality through Christ (Rom. 6:4).

This is the heart of the chapter. Romans 6 is not mainly a call to imitate Jesus from a distance. It is about being joined to Him. Christ died unto sin once, and He lives unto God (Rom. 6:10). Those who are united with Him enter a new relation to sin and to God. The old existence, governed by sin, is not patched up. It is judged in the cross. A new life begins out of the resurrection life of Christ.

Paul says, “our old man is crucified with him, that the body of sin might be destroyed, that henceforth we should not serve sin” (Rom. 6:6). He is speaking about the old life under sin's rule, the old self in solidarity with Adam's broken order. That old mastery has been struck at its root. The purpose is clear: no longer serving sin.

That does not mean every inward struggle vanishes at once. Paul still issues commands throughout the chapter. He still warns against letting sin reign. He still calls believers to present themselves to God. If temptation were gone entirely, those commands would have no force. Being dead to sin does not mean sin never calls again. It means sin no longer has the right to rule the one who belongs to Christ.

That distinction matters. Romans 6 is strong, but it is not careless. Paul does not teach that grace leaves the believer unchanged. Neither does he teach that the believer becomes incapable of moral conflict. He teaches a real break with the old dominion of sin and a real entrance into the life of Christ.

Sin Shall Not Reign

The language of rule and mastery runs through the chapter. Paul is not describing sin merely as a bad habit. He describes it as a power that reigned in death (Rom. 5:21), a master that enslaves (Rom. 6:6, 16-17), and a lord whose wages are death (Rom. 6:23). That is why grace cannot be treated as indulgence. Grace is not a polite attitude toward bondage. Grace is God's act of liberation.

Paul says, “Let not sin therefore reign in your mortal body, that ye should obey it in the lusts thereof” (Rom. 6:12). The word “therefore” matters. Because believers have died and risen with Christ, they are not to allow sin to exercise kingly power over the body. Paul is realistic: the body is still mortal, and sin still seeks expression through it. But he is equally clear: the believer is no longer under sin's rightful dominion.

He continues, “Neither yield ye your members as instruments of unrighteousness unto sin: but yield yourselves unto God” (Rom. 6:13). Sanctification in Romans 6 is not a vague inward feeling. It reaches the concrete parts of life. Mind, tongue, hands, habits, desires, choices, and relationships are all included. Grace comes to claim the actual life of the believer.

Then comes one of the most discussed promises in the chapter: “For sin shall not have dominion over you: for ye are not under the law, but under grace” (Rom. 6:14). In the flow of Romans, this cannot mean that moral obligation has disappeared. The rest of the chapter immediately denies that conclusion: “What then? shall we sin, because we are not under the law, but under grace? God forbid” (Rom. 6:15).

To be “under the law” in this context is not to live under God's moral will as such, but to stand before the law as a realm where sin condemns and exposes without giving power to overcome. The law can reveal sin, but it cannot free the sinner from sin's mastery. Grace does what the law cannot do as a means of righteousness and life. Grace forgives, unites the believer to Christ, and brings him into a new sphere where sin's dominion is broken.

That is why being under grace does not loosen obedience; it makes real obedience possible. Grace is not the cancellation of holiness. Grace is the beginning of holy living.

Servants of Righteousness

Paul now shifts from death and resurrection language to slavery language. Modern readers may find the imagery severe, but Paul uses it because it is morally clarifying. There is no neutral freedom in this chapter. People do not live without a master. The only question is which master they serve.

“Know ye not,” Paul asks, “that to whom ye yield yourselves servants to obey, his servants ye are to whom ye obey; whether of sin unto death, or of obedience unto righteousness?” (Rom. 6:16). This is not an argument for salvation by merit. It is an argument about the moral reality of lordship. One cannot confess Christ and then make peace with sin as a chosen ruler.

Yet Paul does not speak as though the Roman believers were still where they once were. “Ye were the servants of sin, but ye have obeyed from the heart that form of doctrine which was delivered you” (Rom. 6:17). This is one of the richest statements in the chapter. Their obedience is not outward conformity only. It comes “from the heart.” The gospel has not merely informed them. It has claimed them.

Then Paul adds: “Being then made free from sin, ye became the servants of righteousness” (Rom. 6:18). Freedom in Romans 6 is not self-rule. It is not the modern dream of belonging to no one. Biblical freedom is release from sin's tyranny so that one may belong rightly to God. The believer is liberated from one slavery and brought into another, but the second is not degrading. It is life-giving, holy, and joyful because it is service to the righteous God.

Paul acknowledges human weakness in the way he speaks: “I speak after the manner of men because of the infirmity of your flesh” (Rom. 6:19). The point is practical. Just as they formerly yielded themselves to uncleanness and iniquity, now they are to yield themselves unto righteousness unto holiness. Holiness in this chapter is not passive. It is not accidental. It involves real surrender and real moral direction.

The Fruit Then and Now

Paul presses the contrast further by asking what fruit came from the old life: “What fruit had ye then in those things whereof ye are now ashamed? for the end of those things is death” (Rom. 6:21). Sin promises much and pays badly. Its temporary pleasures hide its true harvest. Shame and death stand at the end of its road.

By contrast, those who have been made free from sin and have become servants to God have “fruit unto holiness, and the end everlasting life” (Rom. 6:22). Paul does not oppose fruit and gift. He distinguishes them. Holiness is the fruit of belonging to God. Eternal life is God's gift in Christ. The fruit does not purchase the gift, but the gift never leaves life fruitless.

This is one of the central balances Romans 6 preserves. Salvation is not earned by works. Yet salvation creates a new moral direction. Grace does not merely change a record in heaven while leaving the life untouched. Grace plants the believer in a new realm, under a new Lord, with a new future already shaping the present.

The chapter closes with one of the clearest contrasts in Scripture: “For the wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord” (Rom. 6:23). Wages are earned. Gift is given. Sin pays what is due. God gives what could never be deserved. Yet even that final verse belongs to the whole chapter. It is not only about the end of two roads. It is about two masters, two slaveries, two outcomes, and two kinds of rule.

What Romans 6 Does Not Mean

Grace safely covers deliberate sin

One mistake is to turn grace into a safe cover for deliberate sin. Paul allows no room for that reading. His repeated “God forbid” and his repeated commands show that grace is hostile to sin's dominion. A gospel that leaves a person content under the reign of known sin is not the gospel Paul is expounding here.

Being dead to sin ends temptation

Another mistake is to claim that if believers are dead to sin, then temptation must disappear altogether. But Paul continues to warn, command, and exhort. Sin is not to reign, which means it still seeks to reign. The Christian life includes conflict, vigilance, and surrender to God. Romans 6 does not deny the battle; it denies sin's rightful rule.

Freedom means autonomy

A third mistake is to imagine freedom as autonomy. Paul does not say believers are freed to belong to themselves. He says they are freed from sin and become servants of righteousness and servants of God (Rom. 6:18, 22). Grace does not enthrone self. Grace restores proper lordship.

Romans 6 can be isolated from grace

A fourth mistake is to separate Romans 6 from the larger movement of Romans. If one isolates the commands of this chapter from Romans 3-5, holiness becomes moral strain. If one isolates grace in Romans 3-5 from Romans 6, pardon becomes moral carelessness. Paul refuses both distortions. The justified are also united to Christ, and those united to Christ are called into newness of life.

The Doctrinal Force of the Chapter

Romans 6 matters because it shows that salvation in Christ is not only legal pardon but living union. Paul certainly teaches justification, but he does not stop there. The believer is not only acquitted; the believer is transferred. Not only forgiven; also brought into Christ's death and resurrection life. Not only freed from guilt; also freed from sin's dominion.

This chapter also shows the harmony of grace and obedience. Obedience does not replace grace. Obedience flows from grace. The believer does not obey in order to create union with Christ. The believer obeys because union with Christ has become reality.

It also clarifies the moral seriousness of the gospel. Some imagine that a strong doctrine of grace must weaken the call to holiness. Romans 6 proves the opposite. Paul is able to speak most strongly against sin because he understands grace most deeply. Grace is not less demanding than legalism; it is more penetrating. It reaches the heart, the body, the loyalties, and the future.

Why This Matters for Reading Romans

Without Romans 6, readers may think the main problem in Romans is guilt alone. But Paul shows that the human problem also includes bondage. People need pardon, but they also need deliverance from sin's rule. That is why Romans moves from justification in chapters 3-5 to new life in chapters 6-8.

This chapter also prepares the way for the tensions of Romans 7 and the triumph of Romans 8. Romans 6 announces that sin shall not reign. Romans 7 exposes the weakness of the flesh and the inability of the law to rescue. Romans 8 then opens the life of the Spirit. Read together, these chapters show that victory is neither self-produced nor imaginary. It comes through Christ and the Spirit, and it always rests on grace.

Final Reflection

Romans 6 leaves no room for boasting, despair, or careless living. It humbles, because no one frees himself from sin. It gives hope, because Christ has done what sinners could not do. It calls for decision, because the body, the habits, and the heart are no longer to be yielded to sin. And it gives courage, because the one who belongs to Christ does not stand under the old mastery anymore.

The question at the opening of the chapter still confronts every generation: Shall we continue in sin, that grace may abound? Paul's answer still stands: absolutely not. Grace does not excuse sin. Grace breaks its dominion and brings the believer under the lordship of Jesus Christ, where holiness becomes the fruit of a life made new.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Romans 6 teach sinless perfection?

Romans 6 teaches a real break with sin's dominion, not the end of all conflict or temptation. Paul still commands believers not to let sin reign and to yield themselves to God, which shows the battle remains even though the old mastery has been broken.

What does it mean to be dead to sin?

It means the believer's former relation to sin as ruling master has been broken through union with Christ. Paul is not saying sin has ceased to exist, but that the believer no longer belongs under its dominion.

How does baptism function in Romans 6?

Paul presents baptism as the sign of union with Christ in His death and resurrection. The emphasis in the chapter is not on ritual by itself, but on the reality to which baptism points: burial of the old life and the beginning of newness of life.

What does “not under law but under grace” mean?

In Romans 6, it does not mean moral indifference or freedom to sin. It means believers are no longer under the law as a condemning regime that exposes sin without delivering from it. Under grace, they are forgiven, joined to Christ, and empowered for obedience.

Why does Paul speak about slavery in Romans 6?

He uses slavery language to show that human beings always live under some mastery. The gospel does not create neutral independence. It frees people from slavery to sin and brings them into the life-giving service of righteousness and of God.
