Christ, Law, and Righteousness · Article 18

Does Romans 10:4 Abolish the Law?

Romans 10:4 ends the use of law as a ladder to acceptance with God. It does not erase God's moral will or turn grace into indifference.

Romans 9:30-10:13 Law and Gospel 15 min read

Introduction

Few verses in Romans are pulled out of context more quickly than Romans 10:4: “Christ is the end of the law for righteousness to everyone that believeth.” Read in isolation, it can sound as though Paul is announcing that God's law no longer matters, obedience has been set aside, and the entire moral question has been dissolved in a single sentence. But that is not how Romans works, and it is not what this verse is doing in Paul's argument.

Romans 10:4 stands in the middle of a larger burden. Paul is grieving over Israel. He is not writing as a detached theorist but as a man whose heart is pained by the tragedy of people who possess zeal yet miss the righteousness of God because they seek it in the wrong way. The issue is not whether God's will is holy. Romans has already answered that. The issue is whether sinners can make themselves righteous before God by their own performance. Paul's answer is no. Christ ends that whole path. He ends the use of law as a ladder to acceptance with God.

That does not mean the law is evil, obsolete, or irrelevant. Earlier in Romans, Paul insists that faith does not make void the law but establishes it. Later, he will say that love fulfills the law. In between, he calls the law holy, just, and good. So Romans 10:4 cannot mean that Christ came to sweep away God's moral will. It means something far more searching and far more liberating: Christ is the end of self-righteous striving as the way of obtaining righteousness, and He is the only righteousness for everyone who believes.

The Passage in Context

Romans 9:30-10:13 begins with a contrast that sets up the whole question. Gentiles, who were not pursuing righteousness in the way Israel was, attained righteousness by faith. Israel, pursuing a law of righteousness, did not arrive where it was trying to go. Why not? Because it sought righteousness “not by faith, but as it were by the works of the law.” The problem was not that Israel cared about holiness. The problem was that Israel stumbled over God's appointed way of righteousness in Christ.

That is why Romans 10 opens with Paul's prayer for Israel's salvation. He acknowledges their zeal, but zeal by itself is not saving. There is a form of religious earnestness that remains far from God because it is turned in the wrong direction. Paul describes Israel as “ignorant of God's righteousness” and “going about to establish their own righteousness.” That sentence is one of the keys to Romans 10:4. The question is about righteousness before God. The question is about how guilty people are made right with Him. The question is not whether God still cares about holiness. The question is whether the sinner can produce the righteousness God requires.

Into that setting comes Romans 10:4. Christ is the end of the law for righteousness to everyone who believes. That final phrase matters. Paul does not say Christ is the end of all moral concern. He does not say Christ is the end of obedience. He does not say Christ is the end of God's holy will. He says Christ is the end of the law for righteousness. In this chapter, the issue is law as a means of obtaining right standing before God.

The rest of Romans 10 confirms that reading. Paul contrasts the righteousness that depends on doing with the righteousness that comes by faith. He brings the reader away from self-achievement and toward the nearness of the saving word: Christ has come; Christ has died; Christ has risen; the word is near; salvation is given through the confessed and believed Lord. The entire paragraph moves away from performance as the basis of acceptance and toward faith in the risen Christ.

Main Explanation

Israel's Zeal Without Knowledge

Paul's sorrow in Romans 10 is not aimed at irreligion but at misdirected religion. Israel had zeal for God, yet zeal did not save them. That matters because it exposes a common spiritual mistake. A person may be serious, disciplined, devoted, and even deeply committed to Scripture, and still miss the righteousness of God if he tries to establish his own.

That is the heart of the problem. To “establish their own righteousness” is to stand before God on the basis of what one has done, what one knows, what one belongs to, or how one compares to others. It is the instinct to present a record instead of receiving a gift. Romans has already shown why that effort must fail. By the deeds of the law no flesh shall be justified in God's sight. The law can reveal sin, expose the conscience, and shut the mouth of boasting, but it cannot turn the guilty into the righteous by the sinner's own performance.

So when Paul reaches Romans 10:4, he is not introducing a brand-new topic. He is returning to a central theme of the letter: righteousness before God cannot be secured by human law-keeping.

Christ and the End of the Law for Righteousness

What, then, does Paul mean?

At the very least, he means this: Christ brings to an end the use of the law as the path to righteousness for the believer. Once Christ has come, once His death and resurrection stand at the center of God's saving action, the sinner must stop looking to personal performance as the basis of acceptance. Christ ends that quest because He Himself is the righteousness of all who believe.

This fits the larger movement of Romans. In chapter 3, God reveals His righteousness apart from law as a means of justification, though witnessed by the Law and the Prophets. In chapter 4, Abraham shows that righteousness is counted through faith, not earned through works. In chapter 5, justification brings peace with God. In chapter 10, Paul is still on that same ground. Christ ends the law as a saving method because righteousness is now found in Him, not in the sinner's record.

That does not mean the law was meaningless before Christ, nor does it mean that Moses was opposed to Christ. Paul is not pitting Moses against Jesus as if the Old Testament were one religion and the gospel were another. Romans as a whole refuses that idea. The Law and the Prophets bear witness to the righteousness of God revealed in the gospel. Abraham himself is the display case of faith. The problem is not Moses. The problem is the sinner's misuse of the law.

Moses, Faith, and the Nearness of Salvation

Romans 10:5-10 sharpens the contrast. “Moses describeth the righteousness which is of the law, That the man which doeth those things shall live by them.” Paul is not insulting Moses there. He is showing the character of a righteousness sought on the basis of doing. If righteousness rests on that ground, then the one who seeks it must stand there by doing. But that path cannot justify the guilty.

By contrast, the righteousness of faith does not tell the sinner to ascend into heaven or descend into the deep. It does not demand heroic spiritual feats to bring Christ down or raise Him up. Christ has already come. Christ has already died. Christ has already been raised. The saving word is near because the saving work has been done. Salvation rests not on human ascent but on divine accomplishment.

That is why confession and belief sit at the center of Romans 10:9-10. The believer does not present merit. The believer receives Christ. The mouth confesses Jesus as Lord, and the heart believes that God raised Him from the dead. Salvation rests in the risen Lord, not in a successful attempt to establish one's own righteousness.

Why Romans 10:4 Cannot Mean the Law No Longer Matters

If Romans 10:4 abolished the law in every sense, it would collide with Paul's own teaching elsewhere in Romans.

Romans 3:31 asks, “Do we then make void the law through faith?” Paul's answer is immediate and emphatic: “God forbid: yea, we establish the law.” So faith does not empty the law of meaning.

Romans 7:12 declares, “Wherefore the law is holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good.” So the law itself is not the problem. Sin is the problem. The flesh is the problem. The misuse of law as a means of righteousness is the problem.

Romans 13:8-10 speaks of love fulfilling the law. Commands against adultery, murder, stealing, coveting, and every other commandment are gathered up in the command to love one's neighbor. Love does not abolish those moral claims. Love fulfills them in lived obedience.

Taken together, those passages make Paul's meaning much clearer. Christ does not abolish God's moral will. He abolishes the sinner's hope of being justified by personal law-keeping. He ends law as the route to righteousness, not law as the revelation of God's holy character.

Doctrinal Meaning

Romans 10:4 protects the gospel in two directions.

First, it protects the gospel from legalism. If Christ is the end of the law for righteousness, then no one can stand before God on the basis of record, effort, heritage, zeal, or religious discipline. The law cannot be turned into a saving ladder. The sinner must be brought away from self-establishment and into faith. Christ alone is righteousness for the believer.

Second, it protects the gospel from lawlessness. Because Paul does not say the law is sin, evil, or meaningless, the gospel cannot be turned into moral indifference. The believer is not justified by obedience, but neither is he saved into disobedience. The same Romans that proclaims free justification also speaks of the obedience of faith, the holy commandment, the life of the Spirit, and love as the fulfillment of the law.

This is one of the great harmonies of Romans: the law cannot justify, but grace does not produce contempt for the law. Grace restores the sinner to God, and in that restored relation the will of God is no longer viewed as an enemy. The law can no longer be used for boasting, but it can still expose sin, direct love, and describe the shape of a life pleasing to God.

That is why Christians must be careful with the language of “freedom.” Freedom in Romans is not freedom to ignore God. It is freedom from condemnation, freedom from sin's dominion, freedom from self-righteous striving, and freedom to belong to Christ. Christ frees us from earning righteousness so that we may receive righteousness and walk in newness of life.

Common Misunderstandings

The Law No Longer Matters

This reading cannot survive the flow of Romans. Paul refuses to make faith an attack on the law. He denies law as a means of justification while affirming its holiness and moral goodness.

Paul Opposes Moses

Romans does not present Moses and Christ as enemies. Paul uses Moses within his own argument. The deeper issue is not Moses but the use of law in the hands of sinners trying to establish their own righteousness.

Faith Makes Obedience Unnecessary

Romans never grants that. Faith receives righteousness; it does not abolish holiness. The same Christ who justifies becomes the confessed Lord of the believer. Where Christ is received as righteousness, He is not received as a permission slip for disobedience.

A Cold Doctrinal Formula

The verse is set inside Paul's grief, prayer, and missionary burden. This is not abstract theology. Paul longs for people to be saved from false confidence and brought into the joy of righteousness by faith.

Practical Meaning for Believers

Many Christians still live as though Romans 10:4 were not true. They measure their standing with God by recent performance, spiritual productivity, private consistency, or comparison with others. When they have done well, they feel near to God. When they have done badly, they feel shut out. In practice, they are still trying to establish their own righteousness.

Romans 10 calls for a different posture. Stop seeking acceptance through performance. Receive Christ as righteousness. Rest where God tells you to rest: not in your moral record, not in your efforts, not in your zeal, but in the crucified and risen Lord.

At the same time, do not react by treating obedience lightly. If Christ has saved you from self-righteous law-use, that does not mean God has stopped caring about truthfulness, purity, mercy, justice, worship, or love. It means you now relate to those things as one who belongs to Christ, not as one trying to buy acceptance from Him.

This changes the inner life. It humbles pride because boasting is excluded. It heals despair because righteousness is given in Christ. It steadies obedience because the believer is freed from both self-congratulation and self-condemnation. A Christian who knows Romans 10:4 rightly will neither boast in himself nor make peace with sin. He will cling to Christ and honor the will of God because Christ has become his righteousness.

Why This Matters for Reading Romans

Romans 10:4 is one of those verses that can distort the whole letter if it is mishandled. Read carelessly, it can make Paul sound inconsistent: perhaps he establishes the law in chapter 3, calls it holy in chapter 7, speaks of love fulfilling it in chapter 13, and then abolishes it in chapter 10. But that is not Paul's reasoning. The tension disappears when the context is honored.

Romans teaches a consistent message. The law is good, but it cannot justify the guilty. Christ provides what the law could never give to sinners as a means of righteousness. Faith receives that gift. The justified then live a new life marked by love, holiness, humility, and obedience.

That is why Romans 10:4 should be read alongside Romans 3:31, Romans 7:12, and Romans 13:8-10. Together they guard readers from two opposite errors: trying to be justified by law-keeping, and using grace as a reason to dismiss God's moral will. Paul rejects both. He takes away self-righteousness, but he does not take away holiness. He takes away boasting, but he does not take away obedience. He takes away confidence in the flesh, but he does not take away the beauty of a life shaped by love.

Final Reflection

Romans 10:4 is not the death of holiness. It is the death of self-salvation.

Christ ends the weary, hopeless attempt to stand righteous before God through personal performance. He ends the use of law as a means of obtaining acceptance. He does so because He Himself is the righteousness of everyone who believes. And having brought the believer into peace with God, He does not lead that believer into moral emptiness, but into a life where love fulfills the law and obedience grows out of grace.

So the verse should not make anyone careless. It should make the burdened heart look away from self and fix itself on Christ. Here is where striving ends. Here is where righteousness is received. Here is where the sinner stops trying to establish his own standing and begins to confess, with faith and gratitude, that Jesus Christ is Lord.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does “Christ is the end of the law” mean in Romans 10:4?

It means Christ brings to an end the use of the law as the way of obtaining righteousness before God. Paul's concern in Romans 10 is how sinners are made righteous, not whether God's moral will has ceased to matter.

Does Romans 10:4 teach that the law has been abolished?

Not in the sense that God's moral will is erased or irrelevant. Romans also says faith establishes the law, the law is holy and good, and love fulfills the law. The verse addresses law as a means of righteousness, not law as the expression of God's holy character.

Why does Paul speak about Israel's zeal in Romans 10?

Because zeal alone does not save. Israel's tragedy was not lack of religious seriousness but pursuing righteousness in the wrong way. They sought to establish their own righteousness rather than submit to God's righteousness in Christ.

Is Paul opposing Moses in Romans 10?

No. Paul is not treating Moses as an enemy of the gospel. He is showing the difference between seeking righteousness on the basis of doing and receiving righteousness by faith in Christ.

If righteousness is by faith, why does obedience still matter?

Obedience is not the basis of justification, but it remains the fruit of belonging to Christ. Romans never treats grace as permission for sin. Instead, grace frees believers from self-righteous striving so they can live in love, holiness, and truthful obedience.
