Faith and Promise · Article 10

Abraham and Righteousness by Faith

Romans 4 presents Abraham as living proof that righteousness is received by faith, God's promise rests on grace, and all believers belong to one family in Christ.

Romans 4:1-25 Righteousness by Faith 13 min read

Introduction

Romans 4 stands in the middle of one of Paul's most important turns. He has already shown that the whole world is guilty before God, that no one can be justified by works of law, and that God has revealed His righteousness in Jesus Christ for all who believe. At that point a question rises naturally: if righteousness is received by faith rather than earned by human performance, is this really the way God has always worked? Paul answers by turning to Abraham.

That move is not decorative. Abraham is central to the argument because he stands near the beginning of the biblical story of promise. If Paul can show that Abraham himself was counted righteous by faith, then righteousness by faith is not a late adjustment in God's plan. It belongs to the very structure of His saving purpose. Abraham becomes living proof that sinners do not stand right with God by boasting in what they have done, but by trusting what God has said and what God alone can do.

Romans 4 is also deeply pastoral. It is not only explaining Abraham; it is teaching readers where to rest. It cuts the nerve of self-righteousness. It also gives hope to weak believers, because Abraham's faith was not faith in his own strength. It was faith in the God who makes promises, keeps promises, brings life out of death, and justifies the ungodly. That is why Paul does not leave Abraham in the distant past. By the end of the chapter he brings the same truth directly to believers in Christ. What was written about Abraham was written for us also.

Why Paul Brings in Abraham

Paul opens Romans 4 by asking what Abraham found according to the flesh. That question matters because Abraham could easily have been misunderstood as an example of spiritual achievement. If anyone might seem able to claim distinction before others, Abraham would be a likely candidate. Yet Paul shuts that path immediately. If Abraham was justified by works, then he would have something to boast about—but not before God.

The entire chapter turns on the contrast between boasting and believing. Paul has already said in Romans 3 that boasting is excluded. Romans 4 shows how and why. Abraham's standing with God did not come from a wage paid for labor rendered. Paul uses the simple but piercing comparison of work and gift. A worker receives pay as something owed. But the sinner who does not come claiming wages, and instead believes on the God who justifies the ungodly, is counted righteous by faith. That is strong language, and Paul means it to be strong. He does not clean people up first and then call them righteous because they have crossed a moral threshold. He justifies the ungodly through faith.

That does not mean God treats sin lightly. Romans 3 has already made clear that justification rests on Christ's redemptive death and God's own righteous dealing with sin. Romans 4 builds on that foundation by showing how the blessing is received. Abraham matters because he proves that grace has always ruled out human pride. Even the father of the faithful did not begin with a record to present. He began with God's word and with his own need.

Paul strengthens the point by bringing in David as well. David speaks of the blessedness of the one to whom God counts righteousness apart from works: the one whose sins are forgiven, whose iniquities are covered, and to whom the Lord will not impute sin. That means counted righteousness is not a cold abstraction. It belongs to forgiveness, reconciliation, and blessedness before God. Abraham shows the principle in the language of faith and promise. David confirms it in the language of pardon and mercy. Together they testify that acceptance with God is not earned.

Counted Righteousness and Excluded Boasting

The phrase “counted for righteousness” stands at the center of Romans 4. Paul quotes the Scripture: Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him for righteousness. The force of the argument is not that faith itself becomes some meritorious work by which Abraham earns favor. Paul is excluding merit, not renaming it. Faith is the open hand, not the purchase price. It receives. It trusts. It renounces boasting because it leans entirely on God.

That point matters because “faith” can be misunderstood in two opposite ways. Some reduce it to a mere opinion, a kind of religious agreement with certain truths. Others make it into a subtler form of achievement, as though faith were a heroic inner work impressive enough to secure God's approval. Romans 4 refuses both errors. Abraham's faith is not empty assent, because it is directed toward the living God and His promise. But neither is it a self-generated virtue that earns reward. Paul's contrast between debt and grace leaves no room for that. Where grace rules, boasting dies.

The counting of righteousness should not be treated as fiction, as though God simply pretends that sin is harmless or unreal. Romans 3 has already shown that God remains just while justifying the believer through Christ. Romans 4 shows the blessed human side of that reality. To be counted righteous is to stand before God on the ground of His gracious judgment rather than one's own record. Sin is not ignored; it is forgiven. Guilt is not redefined; it is covered. The blessedness David celebrates is the joy of a sinner who no longer stands under condemnation because God has dealt with sin in His saving mercy.

At the same time, Romans 4 does not separate counted righteousness from a real life of trust and loyalty to God. Paul is not arguing that faith leaves a person unchanged. He is arguing that change is not the basis on which a sinner first comes to stand right with God. The order matters. The justified life begins with grace received, not merit displayed. Once that order is reversed, boasting enters again, and grace ceases to be grace.

Promise Versus Human Ability

Much of Romans 4 is built around one great contrast: promise versus human ability. Paul keeps bringing Abraham back to the promise because promise locates salvation in God's action rather than man's power. Abraham did not become heir of the world through law but through the righteousness of faith. If the inheritance were grounded in law, faith would be emptied and the promise would be nullified. Law, when treated as a system of earning, cannot produce the inheritance. It can expose transgression, but it cannot secure the promise.

That is why Paul says the promise is of faith, that it might be according to grace, so that it may be sure. This is one of the most comforting lines in the whole chapter. If the promise rested on human performance, it would never be sure. It would rise and fall with human weakness. But because it stands in grace and is received by faith, it rests on God's faithfulness. The certainty of salvation does not come from the strength of the human hand but from the reliability of the God who gives.

Paul then lingers over the bodily impossibility surrounding Abraham and Sarah. Abraham considered his own body as good as dead, and Sarah's womb likewise. Yet he did not collapse into unbelief. He did not pretend the weakness was unreal. Faith in Romans 4 is not denial. Abraham faced the impossibility honestly. But he judged God more weighty than the impossibility. He was strengthened in faith, giving glory to God, being fully persuaded that what God had promised He was able also to perform.

That is one of the clearest descriptions of biblical faith in Romans. Faith does not look within for reasons to hope. It looks away from self and rests in God's power and promise. That is why Abraham must never be turned into a moral hero whose inner greatness is the sermon. The chapter honors Abraham's faith, but only because his faith honored God. Its greatness lay in its object. He believed the God who calls things that are not as though they were and who gives life to the dead. In other words, his faith was strong precisely because God was faithful.

Abraham as Father of Believers

Paul also turns to Abraham because Abraham helps explain the unity of God's people. Romans is not merely answering a private question about how one individual can be saved. It is also addressing the relation of Jews and Gentiles in the saving purpose of God. Abraham serves this argument perfectly because his righteousness was counted before circumcision. That sequence matters. If Abraham had been declared righteous only after receiving the covenant sign, someone might argue that justification belongs only inside one marked ethnic community. Paul refuses that conclusion. Abraham was counted righteous while uncircumcised, and later received circumcision as a sign and seal of the righteousness he already had by faith.

In Paul's hands, that means Abraham is father in two related senses. He is father of the circumcised who walk in the faith that marked him, and he is father also of the uncircumcised who believe as he believed. The point is not to erase Jewish identity or flatten the story of Israel. The point is to show that the promise was always larger than one group's privilege. Abraham's story supports the inclusion of the nations not by bypassing Scripture, but by fulfilling what God was doing from the beginning.

This section of Romans 4 protects the church from pride in more than one direction. It keeps Gentile believers from imagining that they are late arrivals to a brand-new religion disconnected from Abraham. It also keeps Jewish privilege from being misunderstood as though outward distinctions could replace faith. The decisive matter is not possession of a sign alone, but participation in Abraham's faith.

That makes Romans 4 a deeply unifying chapter. God has one family of promise, and Abraham stands as father of all who believe. The family resemblance is not found in boasting, but in trust. It is not built on natural strength, but on divine grace. It is not confined to one group alone, because the promise reaches wider than human boundaries. The same God who justified Abraham by faith justifies all who believe.

Faith in the God Who Gives Life

The climax of the chapter comes when Paul turns from Abraham's experience to the believer's own. He says the words “it was counted to him” were not written for Abraham's sake alone, but for ours also. That transition is vital. Paul is not giving a lesson in biblical history detached from the gospel. He is drawing believers into the same saving pattern.

Yet there is an important development. Abraham believed the God who gives life to the dead in relation to the promise of a son. Believers now trust the God who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. The same divine power is at work, but now the promise has come into clearer focus through Christ's death and resurrection. Paul ends the chapter by saying that Jesus was delivered up for our offences and raised for our justification. That is not an afterthought. It gathers Romans 4 into the center of the gospel.

Faith, then, is always Christward in the full Christian sense. Abraham's faith looked ahead in promise; ours looks to the God who has acted decisively in the death and resurrection of His Son. That keeps righteousness by faith from becoming vague spirituality. The faith that justifies is not faith in faith. It is faith in God through Jesus Christ. It clings to the God who raises the dead and who has already vindicated His saving work in the resurrection of Christ.

This also guards the doctrine of justification from becoming thinly legal. Yes, God declares the believer righteous. But He does so in living union with the crucified and risen Christ. The justified person is not merely handed a favorable verdict while remaining unrelated to the Savior. He is brought into the sphere of Christ's redeeming work, into peace, hope, and new life. Romans 5 will develop that further, but Romans 4 has already prepared the way.

Because of that, righteousness by faith should never be preached as bare pardon without transformation. Paul is careful with order: justification comes by grace through faith, not by works. But the God who justifies the ungodly is the same God who raises the dead. His saving work is powerful, not merely declarative in an empty sense. The believer's confidence rests in a living Savior.

Why Romans 4 Matters for Believers Now

Romans 4 remains urgently needed because the instincts Paul confronts are still alive. People still want wages instead of grace. Some want them openly, by presenting moral effort as the basis of acceptance. Others do it more subtly, measuring their spiritual worth by performance, comparison, pedigree, or visible success. Romans 4 ends all of that at the root. If Abraham himself stood righteous only by believing God, then no sinner today can stand before God by self-congratulation.

The chapter also speaks powerfully to believers facing impossibility. Abraham's faith was tested in the realm where hope seemed unreasonable. Paul does not tell that story to produce romantic admiration. He tells it so that readers will trust God when the promise seems to stand against visible weakness. The God of Romans 4 is not limited by dead ends. He works precisely where human ability has run out. That truth meets the guilty conscience, the weary struggler, the church facing weakness, and the believer who has no strength left for self-rescue.

Romans 4 also teaches the church how to think about its own unity. The family of Abraham is not gathered by boasting in fleshly distinctions. It is gathered by faith in God's promise fulfilled in Christ. That should create humility. It should also create welcome. Where righteousness is received as gift, there is no room for spiritual superiority.

Finally, Romans 4 teaches believers how to glorify God. Abraham gave glory to God by being fully persuaded that God would do what He had promised. That means faith is not passive in the shallow sense of being indifferent. True faith actively honors God by taking Him at His word. It rests, but it rests in Someone. It renounces self-salvation, but it does so by clinging intensely to God's promise in Christ.

Final Reflection

Paul brings Abraham into Romans not to distract from the gospel, but to prove it. Long before anyone could boast in privilege, performance, or moral strength, Abraham stood before God as a believer. He received righteousness not as wages, but as gift. He learned that promise is stronger than impossibility because God is stronger than death. And he became father of all who believe because the way of righteousness has always been the way of faith.

That is why Romans 4 continues to steady the church. It humbles the proud, comforts the weak, draws Jew and Gentile into one family of promise, and directs all confidence away from self to the God who justifies the ungodly and raises the dead. In Abraham's story, Paul teaches every believer where to stand: not in boasting, but in grace; not in self-trust, but in faith; not in human ability, but in the God who keeps His word through Jesus Christ our Lord.
