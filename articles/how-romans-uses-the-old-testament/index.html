How Romans Uses the Old Testament

Paul's gospel is not a new religion detached from what came before. Romans presents Christ as the fulfillment of what God had already spoken through the Law, the Prophets, and the Psalms.

Romans 1-15 Old Testament 12 min read

Romans as a Scripture-Saturated Letter

Romans does not present the gospel as a religious breakthrough detached from everything that came before. Paul writes as a man whose mind is full of Scripture. His letter is shaped by the Law, the Prophets, and the Psalms, not merely decorated with a few borrowed lines. He does not preach Christ as though God started over. He preaches Christ as the One in whom God's long-declared purpose comes into the open.

That matters because Romans is often treated as if it were a bare theological system. Readers sometimes enter the letter expecting a debate about faith, law, predestination, grace, or Christian liberty, and they may miss something more basic: Paul argues from Scripture because he believes the gospel fulfills what God had already spoken. When he teaches righteousness by faith, the guilt of humanity, the place of Israel, the inclusion of the Gentiles, and the obedience that flows from faith, he repeatedly shows that these things do not arrive from nowhere. They belong to the one story of God's dealings with the world.

That is why Romans 1:17 is so important. Paul introduces the gospel and then immediately anchors his message in Scripture: "The just shall live by faith." The gospel reveals the righteousness of God, but that revelation is not foreign to earlier Scripture. It stands in continuity with what God had already said. Later in the letter Paul states this even more explicitly when he says that the righteousness of God is manifested apart from the law as a means of justification, yet is still "witnessed by the law and the prophets." Romans will never allow a reader to set Christ against Scripture, grace against promise, or the gospel against God's earlier word.

Romans is a Scripture-saturated letter because Paul believes the gospel is the fulfillment, not the cancellation, of God's revealed purpose. The Old Testament is not obsolete background material in this epistle. It is witness, foundation, pattern, warning, and promise brought to its proper center in Christ.

The Old Testament and the Gospel Theme

Paul's use of Habakkuk at the start of the letter is not accidental. He could have introduced his gospel in many ways, but he chooses to state the theme and then confirm it from Scripture. The righteous live by faith. That line helps readers understand how Paul thinks. He does not treat faith as a novelty. He sees it written into the Scriptural witness itself.

This sets the tone for all that follows. Romans is concerned with the righteousness of God, but not in a cold or abstract sense. God is righteous in His judgments, righteous in His faithfulness, righteous in His saving action, and righteous in the way He justifies sinners through Christ. Scripture had already been pointing in that direction. Paul's appeal to Habakkuk shows that the gospel does not interrupt the earlier testimony. It reveals its true meaning.

The same pattern appears when Paul exposes human sin. In Romans 2:24 he quotes Scripture to show that the unfaithfulness of God's professed people brings dishonor to His name among the nations. The point is sharp and pastoral. Possessing religious privilege does not excuse sin. Scripture had already warned that covenant unfaithfulness among God's people would become a scandal before the world. Paul uses that warning to strip away false security and drive the reader toward a faith deeper than outward privilege.

Then in Romans 3:10-18 Paul gathers a chain of Scriptural statements to show the reach of sin. This is not a casual use of a verse or two. He lets Scripture's many voices speak together. The Psalms and the Prophets testify that sin has corrupted speech, conduct, desire, and the fear of God. Human guilt is not a merely Pauline idea. Scripture had already diagnosed the human condition. That is why Paul can move from the universal problem to the universal need of grace with such force. The Old Testament itself bears witness that humanity stands in need of God's saving righteousness.

This should correct a common misunderstanding. Some imagine that the Old Testament is mainly law while the New Testament is mainly grace, but Romans will not permit such a split. Paul finds both the exposure of sin and the promise of righteousness in the earlier Scriptures. The gospel does not silence the Old Testament. It makes its testimony burn more brightly.

Abraham, David, and Righteousness by Faith

Romans 4 is one of the clearest examples of how Paul uses the Old Testament to explain the heart of the gospel. He turns to Abraham first, not because Abraham is a marginal illustration, but because Abraham stands near the center of God's covenant dealings. If righteousness by faith is truly the way God saves, it must be visible in the life of Abraham.

Paul shows that Abraham was counted righteous through faith, not through works as a ground of boasting. That argument is decisive in Romans because it reaches back before later covenant signs became points of controversy. Abraham stands as the great example that a person is received by God through trusting His word and promise. This does not make obedience unimportant. Abraham's life was one of responsive faith. But Paul's point is that faith receives what God gives; it does not purchase acceptance.

Then Paul joins Abraham with David. David speaks of the blessedness of the one whose sins are forgiven and not reckoned against him. That move is deeply significant. Abraham shows the positive side of righteousness by faith: God counts righteousness to the believer. David shows the relieving side of righteousness by faith: God forgives, covers, and does not count sin against the sinner. Together they form a powerful Scriptural witness. The gospel Paul preaches is not a theological invention. It is already testified by the patriarch and the king.

Romans 4 also shows that Paul does not use Scripture in a flat way. Abraham functions as an example, but more than that, as a covenant pattern. David functions as a witness to the blessedness of forgiveness. Scripture here is not merely supplying proof texts. It is furnishing the very grammar of the gospel. Promise, faith, righteousness, forgiveness, and grace all rise from the Scriptural story itself.

This matters for how Romans should be read. If Abraham and David already testify to righteousness by faith, then the gospel is not opposed to the earlier Scriptures. Nor is justification by faith a narrow doctrine detached from the history of redemption. It belongs to the same saving purpose by which God called Abraham, preserved promise, and taught His people the blessedness of forgiveness.

Scripture in Romans 9-11

Romans 9-11 cannot be understood well without close attention to Paul's scriptural reasoning. These chapters are often approached as a stand-alone discussion of predestination or Israel, but Paul's actual method is to think through the problem of Israel's unbelief and Gentile inclusion by means of Scripture.

He asks whether the word of God has failed, and his answer is governed by the scriptural story of promise, election, mercy, stumbling, and remnant. In this section Paul uses the language and testimony of Moses, Hosea, Isaiah, and Elijah to show that God's purpose has always moved along lines deeper than outward descent alone. The promise has never been reduced to mere physical identity. God's dealings have always revealed both His freedom and His faithfulness.

Hosea becomes especially important in showing that God's mercy reaches where human expectation would not naturally look. Isaiah helps Paul explain both Israel's stumbling and the existence of a remnant. Elijah helps prove that even in times of widespread unbelief God has not left Himself without a people. Paul is not improvising a theory to explain a crisis. He is reading the present situation in light of the scriptural pattern.

That is why Romans 9-11 must not be read as if Paul were abandoning the gospel he has already preached. These chapters defend the righteousness and faithfulness of God. If God's saving word were seen to fail in relation to Israel, the whole message of Romans would be shaken. Paul therefore turns to Scripture to show that God's promise stands, that unbelief is real and serious, that Gentile inclusion is part of God's purpose, and that boasting has no place.

The olive tree imagery in Romans 11 belongs to the same scriptural logic. Gentile believers are not invited to arrogance. They are warned against pride and called to humility. Israel is not to be treated with contempt. God's mercy is the great theme, and His wisdom breaks human self-confidence. Paul ends this whole section in praise because Scripture-guided reflection on God's ways does not end in human mastery. It ends in worship.

Here, more than anywhere else, readers can see that Paul's use of the Old Testament is not mechanical. Sometimes he cites Scripture as direct testimony. Sometimes he appeals to a pattern in God's dealings. Sometimes he shows fulfillment. Sometimes he warns. Sometimes he explains the present by means of the past. The methods are varied, but the conviction beneath them is one: the God who now saves in Christ is the same God who spoke before.

The Nations in the Plan of God

Romans does not use the Old Testament only to speak about sin, faith, and Israel. It also uses Scripture to show that the nations were always within God's saving purpose. This comes through in several places, but it is especially vivid in Romans 10-11 and Romans 15.

In Romans 10 Paul moves through the language of Moses, Isaiah, and other scriptural witnesses to show both the availability of the saving message and the tragedy of unbelief. The gospel is near, proclaimed, heard, and meant to be believed. Israel's refusal does not mean God's purpose has collapsed. Scripture had already spoken of a people who would hear and of others who would provoke Israel to jealousy. The worldwide scope of the gospel is not a late adjustment. It belongs to the unfolding biblical design.

Romans 15 makes this even clearer. Paul presents Christ as the servant of the circumcision for the truth of God, to confirm the promises made to the fathers, and also so that the Gentiles might glorify God for mercy. Then he strings together scriptural witnesses that speak of praise among the nations. That is a remarkable feature of Romans. Paul does not treat the mission to the Gentiles as a departure from Scripture. He shows it to be scripturally anticipated worship.

This protects the church from two errors. One error is to imagine two unrelated saving stories, one for Israel and one for the nations. The other is to imagine that Gentile inclusion somehow erases the earlier promises. Romans resists both. Christ confirms the promises made to the fathers, and the Gentiles glorify God for mercy. Promise and mercy meet in one gospel. Jews and Gentiles are not saved by separate paths, nor are they called into rivalry. They are gathered into one saving purpose in Christ.

That is why Paul's scriptural reasoning serves church unity as well as doctrine. His quotations are not ornamental. They help humble boasting, correct false assumptions, and teach the people of God to see themselves within one gracious plan.

Why This Matters for Reading the Bible as One Story

Romans teaches readers how to read the whole Bible. Paul does not mine the Old Testament for religious slogans. He reads it as witness to God's righteousness, to human need, to the promise centered in Christ, and to the worldwide scope of the gospel. He sees continuity without flattening differences. He recognizes fulfillment without treating the earlier Scriptures as disposable.

  1. Read one story.The gospel is not detached from creation, covenant, promise, prophecy, failure, remnant, and hope.
  2. Let Scripture interpret Scripture.Difficult parts of Romans become clearer alongside Abraham, David, Moses, Isaiah, Hosea, Elijah, and the Psalms.
  3. Preserve law and gospel.The law reveals sin and cannot justify, yet the Law and the Prophets bear witness to God's saving righteousness.
  4. Learn humility.Jews cannot rest in privilege, Gentiles cannot boast over Israel, and sinners cannot justify themselves.
  5. End in worship.The God who spoke before is the God who has acted in Christ and now gathers one redeemed family.

Romans is filled with Scripture because the gospel is not a break with God's earlier word. It is the glory of that word brought to light in Jesus Christ.
