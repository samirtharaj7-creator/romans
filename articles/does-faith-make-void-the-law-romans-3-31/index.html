Law, Grace, and Obedience · Article 09

Does Faith Make Void the Law? Romans 3:31 Explained

Romans 3:31 guards the gospel from both legalism and lawlessness, showing how faith upholds God's law and bears fruit through the Spirit in love.

Romans 3:31 Law and Grace 22 min read

Introduction

Few verses in Romans are as short and as necessary as Romans 3:31. Paul has just said that a person is justified by faith apart from works of law. That statement is so sweeping, so freeing, and so likely to be misunderstood that he immediately asks the question many readers would ask next: “Do we then make void the law through faith?” His answer is abrupt and forceful: “Certainly not! On the contrary, we establish the law.”

That sentence guards the gospel from two opposite errors. One error turns religion into self-salvation. The other turns grace into moral looseness. Paul rejects both. He will not allow the law to become a ladder by which sinners climb into God's favor. Neither will he allow faith to become a slogan that empties God's commands of their meaning. In Romans, faith and law are not enemies. The law cannot justify the sinner, but the gospel does not overthrow the law's holiness, truthfulness, or moral claim. Instead, the gospel puts the law in its proper place and gives believers a new relation to it through Christ and the Spirit.

That matters because Romans is often mishandled at exactly this point. Some readers hear “apart from works of law” and assume that obedience no longer has any real place. Others hear “we establish the law” and treat the law as the basis of acceptance with God. Paul does neither. He speaks of a righteousness God provides, a faith that receives, a law that exposes sin, a Christ who does what the law could not do, and a Spirit who leads believers into a life that answers to God's righteous will. If Romans 3:31 is ignored, the gospel is easily distorted. If it is heard carefully, the relationship between grace, faith, law, and love begins to come into clear focus.

The Passage in Context

Romans 3:31 does not stand alone. It comes at the end of a tightly argued section in which Paul has brought the whole world under the verdict of guilt. He has already said that both Jews and Gentiles are under sin. He has shown that possession of the law does not save those who break it. By the time he reaches Romans 3:19-20, every mouth is stopped and all boasting is stripped away. The law speaks in such a way that human pride cannot survive its judgment. It reveals sin, but it does not provide the righteousness guilty people need.

Then comes the turning point: “But now.” In Romans 3:21-26 Paul announces a righteousness from God manifested apart from law as a means of justification, though fully witnessed by the Law and the Prophets. That matters. Paul is not describing a gospel cut loose from Scripture, nor a saving plan that treats God's earlier revelation as irrelevant. He is showing that God has done in Christ what sinners could never do through their own efforts. Grace has acted. Redemption has been accomplished. God remains just while justifying the one who believes in Jesus. Because justification rests on God's gracious action in Christ rather than on human performance, boasting is excluded.

It is exactly there that Romans 3:31 appears. Paul knows what his teaching might sound like if heard carelessly. If works of law do not justify, does faith empty the law of all force? Has the gospel pushed the law aside? Paul answers before the misunderstanding has time to settle in. Faith does not nullify the law. It establishes it.

The rest of Romans confirms that answer. In chapter 7 Paul can speak of the law as holy, just, and good. In chapter 8 he says that what the law could not do because of human weakness, God has done through His Son, so that the righteous requirement of the law might be fulfilled in those who walk according to the Spirit. In chapter 13 he can summarize the moral life of love in terms of the commandments and say that love fulfills the law. Those statements do not contradict Romans 3. They unfold it.

Why Romans 3:31 Matters

Romans 3:31 matters because it prevents a false reading of the gospel at the very moment the gospel is being proclaimed most clearly. If Paul had ended the section at verse 30, many readers would likely conclude that the law has no further significance once faith appears. Verse 31 closes that door.

The question itself is revealing: “Do we then make void the law through faith?” The problem is not imaginary. It is the natural objection that rises when justification is declared to be by faith apart from works of law. If human beings are not justified by doing, someone will assume that doing no longer matters. If righteousness is received rather than earned, someone will think moral seriousness has been weakened. Paul knows that careless minds move quickly from grace to license. He refuses the move instantly.

His answer shows that the gospel is not anti-law. It is anti-boasting, anti-self-righteousness, anti-reliance on human works as the ground of acceptance before God. The law had already shown its power to expose sin and silence every excuse. Faith does not reverse that. Faith agrees with God's judgment about sin. Faith confesses that the law's verdict is true. Faith does not claim that sin was a small matter. It comes precisely because sin is so serious that only God's action in Christ can save.

In that sense, faith establishes the law by honoring what the law says about God, sin, and righteousness. A gospel that treated God's moral will as unimportant would not establish the law. It would erase its witness. Paul's gospel does the opposite. It upholds the law's truthfulness about human guilt, and it upholds the law's moral seriousness by showing that salvation required the costly work of Christ. The cross does not suggest that commandments were trivial. It shows that sin against God is so grave that redemption could not come cheaply.

Romans 3:31 also matters because it prevents a divided reading of Romans. Some people read Romans 3 as though Paul were pulling down the law, Romans 7 as though he were partly rebuilding it, Romans 8 as though the Spirit replaced it, and Romans 13 as though love made it unnecessary. But the letter is more coherent than that. The law cannot justify. The law is holy. The Spirit enables a life consonant with the law's righteous requirement. Love fulfills the law's moral intent in actual relationships. Romans 3:31 sits at the hinge of that larger harmony.

What Paul Rejects: Law as a Means of Justification

The first thing Paul rejects is not the law itself, but the use of law as a saving method. That distinction is essential. Romans does not say the law is evil. It says the law cannot justify sinners. That is not because the law is defective in its moral nature, but because guilty people cannot produce righteousness by responding to law with fallen hearts.

Romans 3:27-30 makes this plain. Boasting is excluded. Why? Because justification is not secured by a principle of human achievement but by faith. God is one, and He justifies both Jew and Gentile on the same ground. No one enters by ethnic privilege, religious performance, or accumulated merit. This levels the field completely. If the law could justify, there would always be room for human glorying. Some would stand above others. Some would claim superior standing because of their obedience, knowledge, or heritage. Romans shuts all of that down.

Earlier in the chapter, Paul has already said that by works of law no flesh will be justified in God's sight, because through the law comes the knowledge of sin. That statement does not demean the law. It defines its role. The law reveals. It names. It exposes. It brings the conscience under truth. It removes excuses. But revelation of sin is not the same as removal of sin. Diagnosis is not cure. A mirror can show defilement, but it cannot wash. Romans never treats the law as the source of evil; it treats it as the instrument by which sin is unmasked.

This is why returning to law-keeping as the basis of acceptance with God is not a higher form of spirituality. It is a refusal to let grace be grace. It is an attempt to recover grounds for boasting after God has already taken them away. It also places on the law a burden it was never given to carry. The law can demand righteousness, but it cannot produce it in sinners. It can reveal the shape of God's will, but it cannot pardon guilt or create new life. When people use the law as a method of justification, they do not honor it. They misuse it.

Romans is therefore both humbling and liberating. It humbles because it tells the religious person that commandment-knowledge, moral effort, and outward privilege cannot justify. It liberates because acceptance with God does not rest on human success. Faith abandons self-salvation. It reaches out empty-handed for what God gives in Christ. That does not void the law. It simply refuses to turn the law into a substitute savior.

What Paul Upholds: The Law as Holy and Good

Once the law has been removed from the role of justifier, Paul is free to speak of it truthfully. In Romans 7:7-12 he does exactly that. He rejects the thought that the law is sin. Instead, he says the law made sin known. Sin took occasion through the commandment and produced transgression, but the fault lay in sin, not in the commandment. The conclusion is unambiguous: the law is holy, and the commandment holy and just and good.

That language matters because it prevents an easy but false solution to the human problem. If the law were the problem, then salvation could come simply by discarding moral demand. But Paul goes the other way. The law is good. The crisis lies in the sinner, not in God's moral will. That is why Romans 7 is so searching. It does not portray the commandment as harmful in itself. It portrays sin as hostile, deceptive, and destructive, even while using what is good as an occasion for death. The law shines light; sin rebels against the light.

To say that faith establishes the law, then, includes at least this much: faith agrees with God's assessment of the law. It does not accuse the law of being the enemy. It does not blame God's commandment for the ruin caused by sin. It bows before the goodness of what God has spoken.

This also protects Christians from speaking about obedience in a tone Paul never uses. There is a way of talking about grace that makes any strong moral word sound embarrassing or outdated. Romans does not talk that way. The law is not ridiculed. God's commandments are not treated as an unfortunate burden from which the gospel delivers us into a morally undefined freedom. The freedom of Romans is freedom from condemnation, from sin's dominion, from the futility of self-righteousness, and from the flesh's slavery. It is not freedom from the goodness of God's moral will.

At the same time, calling the law holy and good does not place believers back under a covenant of merit. Paul is not rebuilding the old structure of self-trust. He is showing that one may reject legalism without despising the law. A believer who understands Romans should be able to say two things at once without hesitation: the law cannot justify me, and the law is holy, just, and good. Lose either side, and the gospel is distorted.

The Spirit and the Fulfillment of the Law

Romans 8 carries the argument forward in a decisive way. Paul says that what the law could not do, in that it was weak through the flesh, God did by sending His own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh and for sin. He condemned sin in the flesh so that the righteous requirement of the law might be fulfilled in those who do not walk according to the flesh but according to the Spirit.

That passage is crucial because it explains how Romans can reject law as a means of justification without sliding into lawlessness. The problem was never with the law's moral standard. The problem was human flesh, human weakness, human bondage to sin. The law could name righteousness but could not create it in fallen people. God Himself has acted where human effort failed. The Son came. Sin was condemned. The Spirit now leads believers into a new manner of life.

Notice the order. God acts first. Christ accomplishes what sinners could not. The Spirit is given. Then the righteous requirement of the law is fulfilled in those who walk according to the Spirit. The fulfillment is not the cause of God's saving action; it is its fruit. That protects the gospel from legalism. Yet the fulfillment is real. That protects the gospel from antinomianism.

Romans never imagines a salvation that leaves the believer unchanged. The same grace that justifies also transfers the believer into a new realm of life. The Spirit does not free people from condemnation so that they may return comfortably to sin. The Spirit leads them into a life that answers to God's righteous will from the heart. This is why Paul can oppose “works of law” as a basis of justification while also insisting that believers must not walk according to the flesh.

Here Romans 3:31 becomes even clearer. Faith establishes the law not by turning it into the means of acceptance, but by receiving the gospel through which God deals with sin and creates Spirit-led obedience. The law is upheld more deeply in the gospel than it ever could be in mere outward rule-keeping. External conformity can coexist with pride, fear, and self-congratulation. The Spirit presses deeper. He brings a new direction of life, a new mind, a new relation to sin, and a new willingness to obey.

So when Paul says faith establishes the law, he is not calling believers back to self-generated righteousness. He is describing a gospel in which God's moral will is honored, sin is judged, grace is given, and obedience begins to flow from a new life in Christ.

Love, Obedience, and the Gospel

Romans 13:8-10 shows what this looks like in practice. Paul says that the one who loves another has fulfilled the law. He then names commandments concerning adultery, murder, stealing, and coveting and gathers them into the word, “You shall love your neighbor as yourself.” Love does no harm to a neighbor; therefore love is the fulfillment of the law.

This is not a reduction of morality to sentiment. Paul is not replacing commandments with vague warmth. He is showing that the law's moral intent comes to expression in love. Love is not the cancellation of the law but its lived reality in human relations. It refuses injury, selfishness, exploitation, and covetous grasping because love seeks the good of the other before God.

That is why the gospel and obedience cannot be separated. If grace truly reconciles people to God, if Christ truly frees them from sin's dominion, if the Spirit truly dwells in them, then love cannot remain absent. Love is not a decorative extra added after the central work is done. It is one of the clearest signs that the gospel has taken root. And because love fulfills the law, the law has not been voided at all. It has been answered in the sphere of transformed relationships.

There is an important difference, though, between saying that love fulfills the law and saying that love replaces all moral definition. Romans 13 does not float above commandment. Paul names actual commandments and shows that love moves in their direction. Real love does not excuse adultery, violence, theft, or covetousness. It does not call evil good. It does not shrug at harm. Love is moral, concrete, responsible, and neighbor-serving because it is shaped by God's revealed will.

This guards against a common mistake in modern readings of grace. Some speak as though love means freedom from all moral boundaries. Paul's use of love goes the opposite way. Love is the form obedience takes when the heart has been renewed. It is the opposite of selfish desire. It honors the neighbor. It fulfills the law because it reflects the character of the God who gave the law.

The gospel, then, does not leave believers with a bare legal code, nor does it leave them in a moral vacuum. It brings them into a life where love, empowered by grace, becomes the living expression of obedience.

Common Misunderstandings

The law no longer matters

One misunderstanding says that if justification is by faith, the law no longer matters. Romans 3:31 exists to deny that very conclusion. The law still matters because it reveals sin, tells the truth about God's righteousness, and continues to bear moral witness. What changes is not the law's goodness, but the sinner's relation to it. The believer no longer seeks justification through law-keeping, yet neither does the believer despise what God has commanded.

The law is the problem

Another misunderstanding says that Romans teaches the law is the problem. Paul refuses that claim in Romans 7. The problem is sin, operating through fallen humanity. The law is holy, just, and good. A bad diagnosis will always produce a bad gospel. If the law is blamed, salvation will be imagined as release from moral seriousness. Paul teaches instead that salvation is release from condemnation and bondage into a new life that honors God.

Establishing the law means legalism

A third misunderstanding says that “establishing the law” means returning to legalism. But legalism does not establish the law; it misuses it. It turns the law into a ladder for the proud instead of a revelation that humbles the proud. Faith establishes the law by admitting the law's verdict, receiving Christ's saving work, and leading into Spirit-formed obedience. Legalism keeps the shell of obedience while undermining grace. The gospel gives grace first and from that grace brings forth obedience.

Obedience becomes irrelevant

A fourth misunderstanding says that obedience becomes irrelevant once one is justified. Romans will not allow such a split. Those justified by faith are also united to Christ, called to walk not according to the flesh but according to the Spirit, and urged to live in love. Obedience is not the basis of justification, but neither is it optional ornamentation. It is the fruit of grace in real life.

Practical Meaning for Believers

This teaching reaches directly into the conscience. First, it tells us not to use the law for what God never intended it to do. The law can search us, expose us, and stop our boasting, but it cannot be used to build a case for our own righteousness. Every attempt at self-justification eventually hardens the heart. It creates comparison with others, defensiveness before God, and a religion of outward measurement. Romans calls us away from that dead end and into rest in Christ.

Second, it teaches us to honor God's commandments without fear that obedience will somehow compete with grace. When obedience is placed in the right order, it becomes freedom rather than bondage. The believer obeys not in order to create acceptance, but because acceptance in Christ has already opened the way for a new life. That produces humility. It leaves no room for boasting over others. It also produces seriousness. Grace is not casual, because it cost the work of Christ and brings the gift of the Spirit.

Third, it invites believers to examine whether love is actually shaping their conduct. Romans will not let us hide behind theological correctness if our lives harm others, cultivate selfishness, or excuse cherished sin. Love fulfills the law in actual relations—marriage, speech, money, honesty, worship, patience, neighborliness, and the use of freedom. This moves the discussion out of abstraction. Romans 3:31 is not settled merely when a person can explain it correctly. It is settled when grace produces a life that honors God and seeks the good of others.

Fourth, this passage brings comfort. If law-keeping were the basis of justification, no one could stand. But because justification is by faith, believers are anchored in Christ rather than in fluctuating performance. That assurance is not an excuse for indifference. It is the very atmosphere in which holy living becomes possible. People either try to save themselves or they receive salvation and begin learning to walk in it. Romans calls us into the second.

Why This Matters for Reading Romans

Romans becomes far clearer when Romans 3:31 is allowed to do its work. Without it, one might read Paul as if he were abolishing moral seriousness in the name of grace. With it, the whole letter comes into better focus. The argument from chapters 1-3 shows why the law cannot justify. Chapters 4-5 show the freeness and certainty of righteousness by faith. Chapters 6-8 show that grace leads into a new life in Christ and the Spirit. Chapters 12-13 show what that life looks like in love, humility, and obedience. Romans 3:31 stands like a guardrail between those movements, preventing false conclusions.

It also helps readers handle difficult passages more honestly. Romans 7 does not teach that the law is bad. Romans 8 does not teach that the Spirit frees believers from any moral claim. Romans 13 does not teach that love is undefined feeling. The same letter that denies justification by works of law also upholds the law's holiness, looks for its righteous requirement to be fulfilled in Spirit-led lives, and portrays love as its fulfillment in action.

This means law and gospel should not be set against each other as rival messages. The law names the truth about sin and righteousness. The gospel reveals God's saving action for sinners in Christ. The gospel does not erase the law's witness; it vindicates it and answers the human need the law exposed. That is why faith establishes the law. Faith says yes to God's judgment, yes to God's grace, yes to Christ's saving work, and yes to the new life that follows.

Final Reflection

Romans 3:31 saves the gospel from distortion. It tells the proud that they cannot build righteousness through law-keeping, and it tells the careless that grace has not made God's will weightless. The law still tells the truth. It still reveals sin. It is still holy, just, and good. But the law is no savior. God has done what the law could not do through human flesh. He has given His Son, condemned sin, justified the believer by faith, and poured out the Spirit so that a new life may begin.

So faith does not make void the law. It strips away boasting, honors God's moral truth, receives the righteousness God gives, and begins to walk in the obedience that grace creates. That is not a smaller view of holiness. It is a deeper one. It is holiness no longer pursued as a desperate means of self-salvation, but embraced as the fruit of union with Christ. The gospel does not leave the law behind. It puts the law in its right place and, by grace, brings the believer into a life where love fulfills what God has spoken.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Romans 3:31 mean Christians are saved by keeping the law?

No. Romans 3:31 comes after Paul has denied that anyone is justified by works of law. His point is not that law-keeping saves, but that faith does not cancel the law's truth, holiness, or moral witness.

What does it mean that faith “establishes” the law?

It means the gospel confirms rather than destroys what the law says about sin, righteousness, and God's moral will. Faith receives Christ instead of trusting in human merit, and through the Spirit begins to produce a life consistent with God's righteous requirement.

Is the law the problem in Romans?

No. Romans 7 says the law is holy, just, and good. The problem is sin working through fallen humanity. The law reveals sin, but it cannot rescue sinners from it.

If believers are under grace, why does obedience still matter?

Because grace does not only pardon; it also changes the believer's relation to sin. Romans 8 and Romans 13 show that the Spirit leads into a life where God's will is honored and love fulfills the law in practice.

Does “love fulfills the law” mean commandments no longer matter?

No. In Romans 13, Paul names specific commandments and shows that love moves in their direction. Love is not the removal of moral content; it is the lived expression of God's will in relation to others.
