Study Notes

Romans 12:1

Paul begins the practical section of Romans with the word "therefore," and that word carries the full weight of everything he has already said about sin, grace, justification, adoption, mercy, and the saving work of Christ. He is not starting a new religion of moral effort after eleven chapters of gospel. He is showing what the mercies of God look like when they take hold of a human life. The appeal is not built on fear, merit, or pressure, but on mercy. Because God has acted so graciously in Christ, the only fitting response is consecration. Paul says, "present your bodies." He does not speak only of thoughts, emotions, or inward intentions. The body matters because discipleship is lived in real habits, real desires, real speech, real work, real relationships, and real choices. The body is where the surrendered life becomes visible.

The image of a "living sacrifice" is striking. Under the old sacrificial system, offerings were slain and laid upon the altar. Paul says that those who have received God's mercy are to live continually as offerings set apart for Him. This is not the surrender of a moment only, though it may begin in a decisive act. It is an ongoing life of belonging to God. "Holy" and "acceptable unto God" do not mean that the believer first makes himself worthy and then offers himself. They mean that grace claims the life and sets it apart for God's use. What God has justified, He now calls to consecrate. The sacrifice is living because grace does not abolish life; it reclaims it. Every faculty is to be placed in God's hands.

Paul calls this "your reasonable service." The response of surrender is not extreme fanaticism. It is the sane, fitting, intelligent answer to divine mercy. Once the gospel is understood, withholding oneself from God becomes unreasonable. Paul guards against two opposite errors. One is legalism, as though surrender could purchase salvation. The other is a sentimental gospel that receives grace without yielding the life to God. Paul allows neither. The mercies of God do not leave the believer unchanged. They lead him to place the whole self on the altar - not to be destroyed, but to be wholly God's.
