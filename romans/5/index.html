Study Notes

Romans 5:1

Paul's opening "therefore" reaches back into the closing words of Romans 4, where Christ was delivered up for our offenses and raised for our justification. Paul is not beginning a new subject detached from what came before. He is drawing out the first great consequences of justification by faith. Because sinners are counted righteous through Christ rather than through their own merit, peace with God is no longer a wish but a reconciled standing. This peace is something deeper than a passing religious feeling. It is not the thrill of an emotional moment, nor the fragile calm that depends on circumstances going well. It is the end of hostility. We were the ones at war with God. We resisted His will, distrusted His character, and lived in conflict with His government. Justification means that the breach has been healed in Christ.

That peace is objective before it becomes deeply felt. It stands on the finished work of Jesus - His death and resurrection - not on the changeability of human moods. That matters because many sincere people judge their standing with God by the weather of their emotions. Paul points somewhere firmer. Peace with God rests on Christ's accomplishment and is received by faith. Where faith lays hold of Him, the conscience finds rest because guilt has been dealt with.

The verse also keeps Christ at the center. Peace does not come through inward effort, moral improvement, religious activity, or doctrinal precision by itself. It comes "through our Lord Jesus Christ." He is not an accessory to peace but its only ground. The same Lord who justifies also reconciles. The same Savior who forgives opens the way into a new relation with God.

There is practical force here as well. A person at peace with God need no longer live in constant spiritual defensiveness, alternating between presumption and despair. The heart can be honest about sin because Christ has dealt with it. It can obey without slavish anxiety because acceptance is founded in grace. It can pray without pretending because the war has ended. This first verse of Romans 5 therefore places the believer on settled ground: not self-trust, not self-condemnation, but peace through the crucified and risen Lord.
