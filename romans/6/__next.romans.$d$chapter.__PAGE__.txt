5:T830,Paul opens this chapter by confronting a misuse of grace that still appears wherever people want forgiveness without surrender. At the end of Romans 5 he has said that where sin abounded, grace did much more abound. That truth is glorious, but it can be twisted into something monstrous. If grace rises higher than sin, some will ask whether continued sin gives grace more room to display itself.

Paul puts that false logic into words and then exposes it. Grace is not God's excuse for sin. It is God's answer to sin. It does not exist to protect rebellion from judgment, but to rescue people from the very dominion they could not escape. The question is more serious than it first sounds. It is not only the reasoning of open rebels.

It can also be the hidden reasoning of religious people who speak warmly of mercy while making peace with habits, desires, and loyalties that contradict Christ. Whenever sin is cherished because pardon is assumed, this question is alive again. Paul knows that the gospel of free justification can be slandered as morally dangerous unless it is understood in union with Christ.

The grace that pardons the guilty also transfers them into a new realm of life. Chapters 1 - 5 have shown that human beings cannot justify themselves. Chapter 6 shows that the justified are not left unchanged. So Paul begins not with a complicated argument but with a question that reveals the sickness of the heart. Shall we continue in sin?

The form of the question matters. He is speaking about abiding in sin, living there, letting it remain the accepted order of life. That possibility must be challenged at the door of Christian doctrine. Grace is abundant, but it is never morally indifferent. It was purchased at the cost of Christ's death. It comes to overthrow what nailed Him to the cross.

This line therefore serves as a guardrail for the whole chapter. It keeps the gospel from being turned into indulgence. It also keeps obedience from becoming self-salvation, because Paul's concern is still grace. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance.6:T7b6,Paul's answer is decisive: God forbid. Grace cannot be used to keep sin alive, because the believer has died to sin in union with Christ. The wording does not imply believers are beyond temptation, incapable of failure, or no longer aware of the pull of desire. The commands in the rest of the chapter make that impossible. Paul still warns against letting sin reign, which means its pressure remains real. But its rule is no longer rightful. Before grace, sin was not merely a series of bad acts.

It was a reigning power. It held the person under its dominion, shaped the will, darkened the mind, and turned the life inward upon self. To die to sin, then, means a decisive break in relationship, lordship, and direction. The Christian no longer belongs to sin as its servant. Grace has not made sin harmless; grace has ended sin's claim to rule. That is why Paul frames the matter as an impossibility. How can a person who has died to one realm treat it as home? This line also reveals the moral seriousness of conversion.

The gospel does not say, "Remain what you are, but feel safer about judgment." It says, "Christ has entered your death and called you into another life." A person may still struggle fiercely, but struggle is different from surrender. The one who has died with Christ cannot settle comfortably back under the old master and call it grace. Such a life would deny the very meaning of Christ's saving work. There is comfort here as well as warning. Many believers know too much of inward conflict and may fear that temptation itself proves grace has done nothing.

Paul says otherwise. The governing question is not whether sin still seeks influence, but whether it reigns. Death to sin means that the old allegiance has been broken. The believer stands in a new relation to God, to sin, and to self. Grace does not leave sin enthroned. It dethrones it, even where battle still remains. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance.7:T844,Paul now appeals to what baptism means. Baptism is not an empty religious token, but the sign of union with Christ and with His death. The emphasis falls first on Christ Himself. Baptism is into Him before it is into a community, tradition, or visible fellowship. It speaks of belonging to Jesus, being joined to His saving history, and entering the sphere where His death and life become decisive for one's own existence.

That is why Paul immediately says baptism is into His death. The believer is not merely admiring the death of Christ from a distance. By faith he is identified with it. Christ died in relation to sin - not as one who had sinned, but as the sin-bearer and sin-conqueror. To be baptized into His death means that the old life under sin's rule is brought to an end in Him.

Baptism therefore presupposes repentance. A person is not baptized in order to keep the old life intact with a religious label attached. Baptism confesses that the old order must die. This line also helps explain why the image of immersion has such natural force in the passage. Burial and resurrection imagery follow immediately in the next verse, and immersion fits that pattern in a direct way.

Still, the outward act must not be treated mechanically. Water does not save by itself. Ritual without faith cannot unite anyone to Christ. Baptism matters because it expresses a genuine surrender to the crucified Lord and publicly marks a transfer of allegiance. Paul's point is pastoral as much as doctrinal. If the Romans understand what their baptism means, they will see why the question of verse 1 cannot stand.

Those baptized into Christ's death cannot treat sin as though it still defines them. Their baptism declares that something has ended. The old life has no right to continue as master. A Christian may still need daily repentance and renewed consecration, but he cannot honestly regard baptism as compatible with a settled life in sin. Baptism announces that the believer has been joined to the death of Christ, and that changes everything. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance.8:T9dd,Paul moves from death to burial because burial confirms the reality of death. In baptism, the old order is not merely improved; it is marked as buried with Christ. What is buried is not the personality as such, nor the body as though creation were bad, but the old life dominated by sin. The gospel does not rehabilitate the sovereignty of self. It consigns that old regime to the grave with Christ. Yet Paul's sentence is not satisfied with burial.

It presses on to resurrection: "that just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life." The "glory of the Father" points to the powerful majesty and saving action of God. The resurrection of Jesus was not a private reversal of tragedy. It was the Father's mighty vindication of the Son and the beginning of a new creation reality. Those united with Christ are brought into that same resurrection sphere.

"Newness of life" must not be reduced to moral tidying or external improvement. Paul is speaking of a grace-created life under a new lordship. The believer rises from the burial of the old existence not to resume business as usual, but to walk differently because he now belongs to the risen Christ. The word "walk" is important. It suggests direction, conduct, and an ongoing course of life. Resurrection life is not only a future event at the last day; it is also present transformation in thought, desire, and behavior.

This part of the argument therefore holds together past, present, and future. Baptism points back to Christ's death, marks the burial of the old life, and points forward to a life now lived under resurrection power. The Christian is not left in a grave of repentance. Grace brings him out to live under the reign of God. If the old self has been buried with Christ, then clinging to old patterns as though they were normal is a denial of the meaning of baptism itself.

Paul's reasoning is simple and searching. Christ died and rose. You have been joined to Him. Therefore your life cannot remain what it was. Newness of life is not an optional addition to grace. It is where grace leads. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance. The burial-and-rising imagery naturally accords with baptism by immersion: the believer is lowered into the water and raised as a sign of union with Christ's death and resurrection. The rite has no saving power apart from faith, but it publicly confesses death to the old allegiance and a purpose to walk in newness of life.9:T8db,Paul deepens the logic of union with Christ. If believers have been joined to the likeness of His death, resurrection likeness cannot be treated as optional. The Christian hope does not rest on self-improvement, religious exertion, or mere admiration for Christ. It rests on participation in Him. His death is not only an event outside us to be believed; it is the death into which believers are brought. His resurrection is not only a doctrine to affirm; it is the life in which believers share.

"The likeness of His death" refers to this real participation represented in baptism and enacted in conversion. It does not mean a mere imitation, as though believers simply copy His example. It means that through faith they are joined to the pattern and power of His death in relation to sin. What happened in Christ becomes decisive for them. The old life is judged, the old dominion broken, and the person redefined in Him. Paul then speaks with confidence about resurrection.

Because the believer is joined to Christ in death, he will also share in Christ's resurrection likeness. This includes present spiritual renewal. The entire chapter assumes that resurrection life is already at work in those who belong to Christ. They are no longer what they were. Their new life has begun. Still, the wording naturally opens toward the future bodily resurrection as well. The life begun now in union with Christ will not end in the grave, because Christ's own resurrection has already broken that power.

The importance of the sentence lies in its certainty. Paul never says union with Christ's death may perhaps lead to better things. He says it will lead to resurrection likeness. The gospel is not a call to kill sin in the power of the flesh and hope for the best. It is a call to trust the Christ whose death and resurrection define the entire movement of salvation. Death with Him is never the last word. New life follows because He lives.

At the level of discipleship, the statement shifts the focus away from anxious self-measurement and back to Christ. The question is not whether we can generate new life by effort. The question is whether we are united to Him. Where that union is real, forgiveness, renewal, and future hope all flow from the same risen Lord.a:T840,Paul explains what happened to the old life. The "old man" is crucified with Christ - the former self under sin's rule, the whole person as shaped by rebellion, self-centeredness, and alienation from God. Paul refuses to treat sin as a superficial defect that can be managed. He speaks in crucifixion language. The old life had to be nailed to the cross with Christ because sin's dominion is deadly, not decorative.

He continues: "that the body of sin might be done away with, that we should no longer be slaves of sin." The "body of sin" should not be understood as though the physical body were evil in itself. Paul will soon speak of the members of the body as instruments of righteousness offered to God. The problem is not embodiment but the whole person as commandeered by sin.

Under the old order, the body became the arena in which sin expressed its mastery. In Christ, that mastery is broken. The end in view is clear: no longer serving sin. This line carefully joins the objective work of Christ and its practical consequence. The old man "was crucified." That is God's action in union with Christ. But the purpose is ethical and experiential: that slavery to sin might cease.

Paul does not want believers merely to accept a doctrine of forgiveness while continuing to think of sin as an unavoidable ruler. The cross was the decisive judgment on the old regime. The believer is not being asked to domesticate sin, but to recognize that in Christ its lordship has been condemned. There is seriousness here, but also hope. The Christian struggle is not a desperate attempt to overthrow an unbroken tyranny.

The tyranny has been broken in Christ. What remains is real conflict, but not rightful dominion. A dethroned ruler may still threaten and seduce, but it no longer has unquestioned authority. The statement also prevents shallow accounts of sanctification. New life is not produced by merely turning over a new leaf. The old man had to be crucified. That means the gospel strikes deeper than behavior. It addresses identity, allegiance, and lordship. Christ does not simply improve the old self.b:T86f,Death severs the old claim. A servant who has died is no longer under the authority of his master. In that sense, death ends the old bondage. When Paul applies that reality to union with Christ, he means that sin no longer has the right to rule the believer as before. Its dominion has been broken because the believer has passed through death with Christ. The word translated "freed" can also carry a judicial shade, and some readers have seen here a note of acquittal or justification. That is not foreign to Paul's argument, since chapters 3 - 5 have emphasized the believer's new standing before God.

But the flow of Romans 6 shows that Paul is not speaking only in a legal abstraction. He has slavery in view. Freedom here includes release from rule, not merely release from guilt. The justified person is also transferred from one master to another. Yet Paul is not teaching sinless invulnerability. The believer still needs watchfulness, prayer, yieldedness, and the active refusal of sin's rule. What the statement denies is not the presence of conflict but the continuation of sin's rightful mastery. This distinction matters pastorally. A believer who feels the force of temptation may mistakenly conclude that nothing has changed.

Paul says something profound has changed. Death with Christ has altered the relationship. Sin may still attack, but it no longer owns the person who is in Christ. Paul's sentence therefore helps explain why the commands later in the chapter are possible. Believers are told not to let sin reign because sin is no longer their lawful ruler. They are told to yield themselves to God because they are no longer bound to yield themselves to sin. Liberation in Christ is the reason the moral summons of the gospel is meaningful.

There is also a sobering implication. If death with Christ frees from sin, then any return to willing bondage is irrational and tragic. The old chain has been broken. Why live as though it still defines you? Paul is not encouraging self-confidence, but faith in the reality accomplished through Christ. The believer is free from sin's reign because he has died with the One who defeated it.c:T806,Death with Christ is never an end in itself. The Christian life is not a spirituality of negation, as though the gospel merely strips away sin and leaves emptiness behind. Those who died with Christ also live with Him. The One with whom we died is also the One with whom we live. The phrase "we believe" is important. Christian living remains an act of faith from beginning to end. Union with Christ is not sustained by self-generated moral energy. It is trusted, received, and walked out by ongoing dependence on Him. Sanctification is therefore not independent development after justification. It is the continuation of life in Christ. The believer lives because Christ lives, and because faith clings to that reality.

The future sense of this line should also be heard. Paul speaks of living with Christ in a way that embraces both present participation and future completion. Already believers share resurrection life. They are alive to God and called to present themselves to Him. But the fullness of that life awaits the resurrection of the body and final deliverance from mortality. Because Christ lives beyond death, those joined to Him will live beyond death as well. The statement gives the Christian life its center and its tone. The center is Christ, not self-improvement. The tone is confidence, not self-exaltation. Paul is not saying that believers possess life in themselves. He is saying that they belong to the living Christ.

All hope for present holiness and future glory rests there. For daily discipleship, this corrects two opposite mistakes. One is despair, which looks only at remaining weakness and forgets Christ's life. The other is self-reliance, which talks about holy living as though it comes from disciplined flesh. Paul's language allows neither. Dying with Christ and living with Him define the whole Christian existence. To live the new life is to live from Him, with Him, and toward Him. That is why a justified believer cannot be content with continued life in sin. He has been brought into another life altogether.d:T7c0,Paul grounds Christian confidence in the finality of Christ's resurrection. The risen Christ "dieth no more," and death has no more dominion over Him. The resurrection of Jesus was not a temporary reprieve. He did not return merely to the same vulnerable mortal condition. Death's rule over humanity met its conqueror in Him. Having risen, He stands beyond death's dominion forever. This truth matters directly for the argument of Romans 6.

Believers live with Christ, not with a Savior whose victory is incomplete or reversible. If Christ could again come under death's power, Christian assurance would collapse. But Paul insists that death has no further claim on Him. The enemy that once seemed to triumph at the cross has been decisively defeated in the resurrection. The reign of death introduced through sin has been broken by the risen Lord.

The phrase "has dominion" echoes Paul's concern throughout the chapter with ruling powers. Sin reigned. Death reigned. But death does not reign over Christ. That means the life believers receive in Him belongs to a new order. Their hope is not anchored in their own fragility, but in the permanence of Christ's resurrection life. The wording does not imply Christians stop being mortal in the present age, nor that they cease to struggle.

It means that the Lord to whom they are joined is no longer under the tyranny of the old kingdom. There is also strong pastoral comfort here. Many believers are haunted by the instability of their own hearts, by weakness, relapse, fear, and the shadow of death itself. Paul points away from the self and toward the risen Christ. The decisive fact is not how strong we feel, but who Christ is now.

He has been raised. He dies no more. Death has no dominion over Him. If our life is tied to One who is beyond death's dominion, then sin must no longer be treated as though it still owns the future. The Christian's new life stands under the authority of the risen Lord, and His victory is final.e:T8d0,Paul is careful in how he speaks about Christ's death and life. Christ did not die to sin as though He had ever personally been a sinner. He died in relation to sin as the sin-bearer, the One who entered fully into the judgment and burden of human evil in order to overthrow it. His death was directed toward sin's claim, sin's penalty, and sin's dominion. And it happened once for all. Nothing can be added to it, repeated beyond it, or improved upon it.

This finality is central to the gospel. Christ's sacrifice is complete. The believer does not move on from the cross to another basis of acceptance. Nor is Christ's work an unfinished arrangement awaiting human merit to complete it. His death was once, sufficient, decisive, and effective. That is why continued sacrifice for sin is unnecessary. The cross is not provisional grace. It is accomplished redemption. Paul then draws attention to Christ's present life: "the life that He lives, He lives to God." Resurrection is not mere continued existence.

It is Godward life in perfect communion, obedience, and victory. The risen Christ lives wholly unto God, and that becomes both the pattern and the power of the believer's new life. Christian holiness is not merely abstaining from wrong. It is living unto God through Christ. The wording therefore joins the finished work of Christ with the ongoing life of Christ. His once-for-all death is the basis of forgiveness and freedom from sin's claim. His living unto God is the source from which believers now live.

The gospel never separates pardon from transformed existence. Christ forgives by His death and governs by His life. There is great reverence required here. We must not speak of Christ's relation to sin in a way that suggests moral compromise in Him. Nor should we speak of His life unto God in a way that turns holiness into a cold abstraction. His death shows the seriousness of sin and the cost of grace. His life unto God shows the beauty of divine fellowship restored.

Those joined to Him are drawn into both realities: the old relation to sin has ended, and a new Godward life has begun. That is why grace can never be permission for continued rebellion. It joins us to the Christ who died unto sin once and now lives unto God.f:T897,Having declared what is true in Christ, Paul calls for the response of faith. To "reckon" is not to imagine something unreal, nor to engage in pious self-deception. It is to count as true what God has made true in Christ. Faith does not create the reality; it receives and lives from it. The believer is called to think in line with the gospel rather than in line with the old dominion. There are two sides to this reckoning. First, count yourselves dead to sin. That means sin is no longer to be treated as rightful king. It may tempt, accuse, entice, and press for rule, but it is not the believer's master anymore. Second, count yourselves alive to God. Christian faith is not merely the refusal of evil.

It is the acceptance of a new life directed toward God, sustained by Christ, and open to His purposes. Paul will not let death-to-sin be interpreted in a merely negative way. It is part of a larger resurrection existence. This command is deeply pastoral. Many people live below the level of gospel truth because they keep defining themselves by the old self, the old patterns, the old failures, or the old slavery. Paul tells them to reckon differently - not according to wishful thinking, but according to Christ. The believer must cease granting psychological lordship to what God has judged at the cross. He must also stop living as though communion with God were foreign to him. In Christ, he is alive unto God. Yet Paul is not urging triumphalism.

Reckoning is necessary precisely because the Christian experience still involves conflict. The believer must consciously live by what is true in Christ, not by the shifting impressions of temptation or discouragement. Nor is this defeatism. Paul never says, "Admit sin still rules you and hope for mercy." He says, "Count as true what Christ has accomplished." The phrase "through Jesus Christ our Lord" guards everything. We are not dead to sin by temperament, culture, willpower, or religious habit. We are dead to sin and alive to God only in Christ. That keeps the command both humbling and hopeful. Everything rests in union with Him, and everything flows from Him. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance.10:T7dd,Because the believer is dead to sin and alive to God, Paul now issues a command. The "therefore" matters. Christian obedience grows out of what God has already done, not out of an attempt to create salvation by moral effort. Yet the command is real. Grace does not remove the necessity of active resistance. Sin's dominion has been broken, but believers must still refuse its renewed claim to rule.

The word "reign" is decisive. Paul knows sin still seeks mastery. The question is not whether temptation exists, but whether temptation becomes government. The mortal body remains the sphere of conflict. The body is not evil in itself; it is mortal, vulnerable, and capable of being used either in obedience to God or in service to sin. Paul does not teach contempt for the body. He teaches that the body must no longer be surrendered to the disordering power of sinful desire.

The phrase "its lusts" refers to desires that have become instruments of rebellion. Desire itself is not always sinful, but in fallen humanity desire becomes twisted and self-directed. Sin reigns by getting the person to obey those desires against God. That is why the wording links reign and obedience. What rules is revealed by what is obeyed. Paul's sentence keeps Christian teaching realistic. Paul never says believers have no more struggle because they are under grace.

He says they must not let sin reign. That means grace empowers resistance, not passivity. The Christian life is not a sleepy surrender in which one assumes holiness will happen without deliberate yielding to God. Nor is it harsh self-reliance. The command stands on the foundation of union with Christ. There is also dignity here. That refusal will involve what one watches, rehearses, excuses, feeds, and permits. The body, habits, appetites, and daily choices must come under the lordship of Christ. Sin still seeks a throne in the mortal life. Paul commands believers not to offer it one. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance.11:T5ad,Paul continues the same thought in more concrete terms by calling believers to stop presenting their members as instruments of unrighteousness and to present themselves to God. The members are the capacities of the human person - hands, eyes, tongue, imagination, mind, sexuality, strength, time, and energy. Sin needs instruments through which to act. God also claims the human person for His service. The question is not whether these capacities will be used, but to whom they will be yielded.

"Present" is a word of availability and surrender. One may hand oneself over to sin, consciously or unconsciously, by permitting the body and mind to become channels of unrighteousness. A person can also hand himself over to God. Paul is not describing a merely inward spirituality detached from daily life. Sanctification reaches the practical use of the whole person. What we say, where we go, what we look at, what we nurture, what we do with our strength - all of it belongs here.

The positive side is especially important: present yourselves to God "as being alive from the dead." Christian obedience is resurrection obedience. The believer is not trying to make himself alive by presenting himself to God. He presents himself because grace has already brought him from death to life. That keeps surrender from becoming legalistic striving. It is the response of a person who has been reclaimed, not the desperate effort of one hoping to be accepted.12:T9cf,This part of the argument combines promise and explanation. First the promise: sin shall not have dominion. Paul does not merely say sin ought not rule you, though that is true. He announces that under grace sin's mastery is broken. This is not a promise of effortless perfection but of a real change in lordship. The believer is no longer fated to remain under sin's commanding power. Then comes the explanation: "for you are not under law but under grace." Paul cannot be teaching that God's moral law has become irrelevant, because the entire chapter assumes that sin still means lawlessness and that obedience still matters.

To be under law is to stand before God on the ground of law as condemning authority and as a supposed route to righteousness. The law can reveal sin, define transgression, and pronounce guilt. It cannot forgive, justify, renew the heart, or break sin's dominion. To be under grace is to live under God's saving work in Christ. Grace covers the guilty with Christ's righteousness, removes condemnation, and brings the believer under a new power.

What the law could not do because of human weakness, grace does by uniting the believer to Christ and bringing the Spirit's life into the heart. That is why sin loses dominion under grace. Grace is not indulgence. It is effective deliverance. This line also preserves the harmony of law and gospel. The law remains morally serious and truthful. Grace does not contradict that truth. It answers the law's condemnation by Christ's sacrifice and answers the law's righteous demand by Christ's life in the believer.

A person outside grace remains under condemnation and therefore under the dominion of sin. A person under grace is forgiven and empowered, and therefore no longer belongs under sin's rule. Paul uses it as a reason obedience becomes possible. That is the exact opposite of moral looseness. Grace does what legal striving never could. It ends sin's reigning power and brings the believer into a new relation to God, where the law is no longer an executioner, but the will of the Father now loved through Christ.

Within Christian practice, the wording matters because it keeps grace and obedience in their biblical order. "Not under the law" means the believer no longer stands under the law as a condemned sinner trying to earn life; it does not mean the law has lost its holy witness. Under grace, sin loses dominion, Christ becomes Lord, and obedience becomes the grateful fruit of a life no longer ruled by condemnation.13:T884,Paul raises the objection again in a slightly different form. If believers are not under law but under grace, may they treat sin as harmless? His answer is again decisive: God forbid. The repetition shows how persistent this misunderstanding is. Human hearts either want to turn grace into permission or turn obedience into earning. Paul refuses both errors. The logic being rejected here is subtle. If believers are no longer under the law as a condemning arrangement, might that mean they are now free to ignore God's will?

Paul's answer is not merely that such behavior is undesirable. It is incompatible with grace itself. Freedom from condemnation is not freedom for rebellion. Grace changes the believer's status before God and his relation to God. It does not remove God as Lord; it restores the believer to Him. This is why the gospel cannot be reduced to a change of verdict only. If justification were detached from union with Christ, the objection might seem stronger.

But Paul has already shown that believers died with Christ and live with Him. The statement also has pastoral force because many people do not deny grace with their lips, yet they quietly use it to postpone repentance. They assume mercy while planning disobedience. Paul's answer must be heard there too. Grace never commissions sin. It rescues from sin's claim and teaches the soul to hate what crucified Christ. So the second question of Romans 6 sharpens the first.

Grace is not an umbrella beneath which rebellion may be sheltered. It is the reign of God's favor in Christ, and that reign is holy. The point is that the believer is no longer trying to earn acceptance and no longer dying under condemnation. Grace gives a new heart-direction, and that direction cannot honestly ask for leave to sin. This second question is Paul's own guardrail against an antinomian reading of grace. If "under grace" meant freedom to sin, Paul's question would make sense as a conclusion. Instead, he rejects it with "God forbid." Grace ends law-keeping as a basis of acceptance, but it also ends sin as the believer's chosen master. The Christian is not saved by obedience; he is saved into a new obedience.14:T7de,Paul introduces servant imagery, not to approve human bondage, but to explain the seriousness of spiritual allegiance in terms his readers would immediately understand. No one stands in a neutral moral space. A person becomes the servant of the power to which he yields. Repeated surrender is not accidental; it is allegiance. Paul identifies only two masters: "sin leading to death, or obedience leading to righteousness." The severity of the contrast must not be softened. Sin is not a harmless liberty. It is a lord whose path ends in death. It leads to righteousness because a life surrendered to God begins to take the shape of God's will. This line is especially important because modern people often speak as if freedom means being answerable to no one.

Paul would call that an illusion. The human person is always being formed by what he obeys. Desire obeyed becomes habit; habit becomes character; character reveals mastership. Yield yourself to sin, and sin becomes your lord. Yield yourself to God, and obedience begins to show His righteous work in you. Notice that obedience here is not cold externalism. In the flow of the chapter it belongs to grace, union with Christ, and being alive to God. Paul does not set obedience against faith. He treats obedience as the inevitable expression of faith's allegiance. Someone is being obeyed in every life; the question is who. There is also a warning against playing with sin while imagining one remains untouched.

Paul's wording suggests that what one presents oneself to repeatedly will shape what one becomes. Sin is not content to visit; it seeks servitude. Alongside this, this line offers hope. If yielding creates slavery, yielding to God opens the path of righteousness. The one set free by Christ is not condemned to the old obedience. He may now present himself to another Lord. The gospel restores true moral agency by transferring the believer from the realm where sin dominated to the realm where obedience to God becomes possible and proper.15:T7ea,Paul breaks into thanksgiving because the change he describes is not imaginary. The Roman believers really had been servants of sin. Their past was not a respectable halfway house between rebellion and holiness. They belonged to sin. Yet that is no longer their defining condition. God has acted, and Paul thanks God because grace is the source of the change. The phrase "obeyed from the heart" is central. True obedience is not mere outward conformity, social religion, or fearful performance. It arises from the center of the person.

In biblical terms, the heart involves mind, will, affection, and moral direction. When the gospel is received rightly, it does not merely alter behavior while leaving the heart rebellious. It creates inward consent to the truth. The soul begins to love what it once resisted. Paul also refuses to separate teaching from transformation. The believers obeyed "that form of doctrine." The gospel has a pattern, a shape, a recognizable content. Grace is not vague spirituality. It comes through truth about Christ, sin, righteousness, death, resurrection, and faith.

Yet doctrine here is not handled as information only. It is something to be obeyed. Truth received into the heart reshapes the life. The gospel both informs and forms. The wording also suggests that the believers were delivered over to this pattern of teaching. In other words, they did not remain autonomous judges over the gospel. Grace placed them under it. They were brought into a mold that now shaped them. That is the opposite of modern religious individualism, which treats truth as something to sample without surrender.

Paul envisions a truth that claims the whole person and produces heartfelt obedience. The wording therefore resists two opposite mistakes. One is anti-doctrinal religion, where sincerity is praised but truth is optional. The other is lifeless orthodoxy, where correct statements are prized but the heart remains untouched. Paul knows nothing of either. The gospel must be obeyed from the heart.16:T46f,Paul is not embarrassed by the paradox of Christian freedom. Freedom from sin does not mean autonomy from all authority. It means deliverance from the tyranny of sin so that the person may belong to righteousness. If freedom is defined merely as self-rule, then Paul's language sounds restrictive. But if sin really is slavery, then release into righteousness is the deepest freedom a human being can know.

To be set free from sin means more than being excused from punishment. It means the old dominion has been broken. The believer is not now floating in a moral void with no master. He has been transferred into another service. "Slaves of righteousness" sounds severe to modern ears, yet Paul intends it as life-giving. Righteousness here is not a mechanical standard detached from God.

It is life shaped by God's own holy order. To belong to righteousness is to belong to what is true, clean, just, and fitting for creatures made in God's image. The wording also preserves the right relation between justification and sanctification. Freedom from sin is grounded in Christ's saving work, not in the believer's moral achievement.17:T99a,Paul pauses to acknowledge the limitation of his illustration. He speaks after the manner of men because human weakness often needs blunt and familiar images to understand spiritual realities. The weakness of the flesh here points to the limitations of fallen understanding. More refined language might be possible, but it might not be as clear. Paul wants clarity more than polish. He then states the comparison: "For just as you presented your members as slaves of uncleanness, and of lawlessness leading to more lawlessness, so now present your members as slaves of righteousness for holiness." Sin is progressive.

Lawlessness leads to more lawlessness. A life repeatedly yielded to impurity does not stay where it began. Habits deepen, shame hardens, and the will becomes more accustomed to disorder. Sin disciplines its servants in evil. Paul uses that tragic momentum to call for a new momentum. Just as people once gave themselves energetically to sin, now they are to give themselves to righteousness. Christian consecration is not passive. The members once surrendered to uncleanness must now be surrendered to righteousness.

The same seriousness of yielding is required, but the direction has changed. The goal is "holiness." Holiness should not be imagined as cold superiority or spiritual stiffness. In this context it is the beautiful result of belonging to God. It is the life gradually brought into harmony with His character. Because God is pure, truthful, loving, and just, holiness means increasing resemblance to Him. It is not an artificial religious style but moral and spiritual likeness to the One who saves.

This part of the argument also reminds us that sanctification is embodied. The members matter. One does not pursue holiness only in thought while leaving the actual life untouched. The body, habits, appetites, and practical conduct must be yielded to righteousness. That is why the Christian life requires discipline, vigilance, and deliberate offering of self to God. Yet this part of the argument does not make holiness the basis of acceptance. The call to present the members arises within a chapter rooted in union with Christ and life under grace.

Holiness is where righteousness leads. It is the fruit of new allegiance. Paul's human illustration, then, makes one thing plain: the Christian life is not static. Either lawlessness deepens, or holiness grows. The one who belongs to grace is called to the second path.18:T8d8,Paul describes the old life with sharp irony. There was a kind of freedom, but it was freedom from righteousness. It was a liberty stripped of what makes human life right. Sin often presents itself as emancipation from constraint, from accountability, from the will of God. Paul unmasks that claim. To be free from righteousness is not noble independence. It is estrangement from the very reality for which human beings were made. Paul's sentence therefore exposes the emptiness of sinful liberty.

Under sin's rule, a person may feel autonomous, but he is actually cut off from what accords with God's character and order. He is "free" only in the sense that righteousness has no governing place in his life. That is not the flowering of personhood. It is its corruption. The soul detached from righteousness is not in open space; it is in slavery disguised as freedom. The statement also helps Christians interpret their former life honestly.

There may have been pleasures, ambitions, thrills, pride, or social admiration in it, but it was free from righteousness. It bore no true harmony with God's will. It could not produce the fruit of holiness. It may have appeared alive, but it lacked the life that matters most. That memory serves a pastoral purpose. Believers must not romanticize the old existence. Grace trains the mind to recognize that what once seemed free was actually barren and enslaving.

Paul is also preparing for the contrast of verse 22. Freedom always has an object. There is no morally neutral liberty. Under sin, one is free from righteousness. In Christ, one is free from sin. Which freedom is true life? Paul's whole argument answers that question. Within Christian practice, this part of the argument is searching because modern culture often treats freedom as the highest good without asking what the self is free for. Paul would insist that no life is truly whole when it is free from righteousness.

Every liberty that normalizes alienation from God is self-destructive. It may feel expansive for a while, but it narrows the heart and empties the life. The Christian should read Paul's sentence not as contempt for the past only, but as a continuing warning: never call it freedom to be ungoverned by what is righteous before God.19:T738,Paul asks believers to evaluate the old life by its harvest. Sin must not be judged by its first appearance, its momentary pleasure, or its promises, but by its fruit and its end. The first answer is shame. Paul is not trying to crush repentant believers under endless humiliation. Shame here is truthful moral recognition. It is the straightened mind and conscience finally seeing the old life for what it was.

What once may have been defended, enjoyed, or even celebrated is now seen as demeaning, corrupting, and unworthy of a life made for God. Grace does not erase moral memory in a shallow way. It teaches the believer to look back honestly and say: those things were not worthy of the life to which I have now been called. The second answer is death.

Sin's fruit is not merely regret. Its end is death. That is the direction toward which it naturally grows. Because sin separates from God, and God is the source of life, death is not some exaggerated penalty tacked on from outside. It is the true end of a life bent away from God. Paul has already said that death reigns through sin. Here he presses that truth personally.

Those former things were moving toward death all along. The wording is powerful in the moral life because temptation tends to isolate the present from the outcome. Sin whispers about immediate satisfaction while hiding its harvest. Paul drags the whole process into view. What fruit did it have? What end did it carry? Those questions unmask sin better than mere rule-keeping ever could, because they expose the truth of its nature.

For believers, this honest remembering serves mercy. It helps prevent return. Grace does not teach us to admire what Christ died to save us from. It teaches us to see the old life without sentimentality and without despair. Its end was death, and only grace interrupted that road.1a:T6c0,Paul places the present life of the believer in bright contrast to the former life. The rule of sin, the shameful fruit, and the end of death no longer define the one who is in Christ. Grace has brought a real transfer of lordship. The believer has been set free from sin and has become the servant of God. Again Paul joins freedom and service. To modern ears that may sound contradictory, but in Paul's theology it is the very meaning of redemption. Freedom from sin is not the absence of all claim on the life. It is restoration to the claim of the true Lord.

And that new belonging has fruit. Unlike the barren shame of the old life, this new life bears fruit "to holiness." Holiness here is not presented as self-generated moral polish or the purchase price of heaven. It is fruit. It grows from relationship, allegiance, and divine life at work within the believer. The argument preserves the gospel from two distortions. It protects against legalism, because holiness is not made the basis on which eternal life is earned. It protects against moral indifference, because freedom from sin does not remain invisible. It bears fruit. The life that belongs to God begins to take on the character of God.

The person grows in purity, truth, self-control, love, reverence, and practical obedience. Paul then names the end: everlasting life. The word "end" here is not wages paid for human merit, but the goal toward which the grace-governed life is moving. Eternal life is still God's gift, as the next verse will make explicit. Yet the road to that gift is not fruitless. It is marked by holiness. This is why the New Testament can speak of holiness with great seriousness without ever turning it into the ground of salvation.1b:T9eb,Paul closes the chapter with one of Scripture's clearest contrasts: sin pays wages, but God gives a gift. The contrast is exact and devastating. Wages are earned. They are what a master owes for service rendered. Sin is such a master. It keeps people at work under its commands, often hiding the true salary until the end. But the payment is not life, pleasure, freedom, or fulfillment. It is death. Sin always pays according to its own nature, and death is what lies within it. That statement should not be heard lightly. Death is not only physical dissolution, though it includes that. It is the whole ruin brought by separation from God. Sin carries death because it tears the creature away from the Creator, and there is no life apart from Him. So Paul is not describing a cruel overreaction on God's part. He is telling the truth about what sin is and what it produces.

Sin pays what it has always contained. Over against that stands "the gift of God." Eternal life is not wages. It cannot be earned by moral effort, religious ceremony, spiritual lineage, or even the believer's later obedience. It is gift from beginning to end. Paul could hardly choose a stronger word to defend grace. Where sin deals in earned payment, God deals in undeserved generosity. The sinner brings guilt and need; God gives life. Yet the gift is not abstract. It is "through Jesus Christ our Lord." Eternal life does not come through spiritual principle, inner optimism, or human goodness. It comes only through the crucified and risen Christ. Everything in Romans 6 has been moving to this point. Death with Christ, life with Christ, freedom from sin, surrender to God, holiness, and hope - all are bound up in Him. Apart from Him, sin pays its wages.

In Him, God gives what sinners could never buy. This final verse also gathers the whole moral vision of the chapter. Grace is not permission for sin because sin's master always pays in death. Grace is God's gift of life in Christ, and that gift carries with it a transfer of lordship, a new walk, and a holy direction. The chapter ends, fittingly, not with the strength of the believer but with Christ: eternal life is God's gift through Jesus Christ our Lord. Paul's gospel is more radical than permission; it is deliverance. Eternal life is not a naturally immortal possession of the human soul. God alone possesses immortality inherently and grants eternal life as a gift in Christ.
