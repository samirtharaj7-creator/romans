Study Notes

Romans 7:1

Paul opens with a principle his readers can recognize: law exercises authority over a person as long as that person lives. He is speaking to people who "know the law," and the point is not controversial. Law addresses the living. That simple truth becomes the doorway into the larger argument he has been building since chapter 6. He has already said that believers died with Christ, that the old life came under judgment in His death, and that sin is not to rule those who are under grace. Now he asks what that death means in relation to law. He is not attacking the law of God. He is explaining how death changes a person's relation to a legal claim that stood over him. The law itself remains what it is; the question is how a sinner, condemned and powerless in himself, can stand in a new relation to it through union with Christ.

The word "dominion" matters here. In the surrounding context Paul has spoken of sin and death as tyrants. Here he says law has authority while one lives. In the case of the sinner, law speaks with condemning force because it identifies sin and announces guilt. In that sense, to be "under the law" is not merely to know its commandments but to stand before it as one whose old life remains unbroken and whose conscience cannot escape its verdict. Paul is preparing his readers to understand that death with Christ is not release into moral independence. It is release from the old situation in which law confronts the sinner only as a condemning power. That keeps this part of the argument from being misread in two opposite directions. On one side, it does not teach that the law is the enemy and must be cast off.

On the other side, it does not suggest that law can rescue the sinner by demanding better performance. Law rules where life remains unchanged. It can name the good, expose the bad, and hold a person accountable, but it cannot raise the dead or create obedience in a heart still governed by the flesh. Paul begins here because until that is understood, grace will either be feared as lawlessness or misused as permission to sin. Genuine freedom begins where the old life has met the death of Christ.
